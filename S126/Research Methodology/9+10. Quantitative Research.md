## 1. Definition & Overview

### What is Quantitative Research?

> **"Explaining phenomena by collecting numerical data that are analysed using mathematically based methods (in particular statistics)"**
> — Aliaga & Gunderson, 2000

*Giải thích hiện tượng bằng cách thu thập dữ liệu số và phân tích chúng bằng các phương pháp toán học.*

More formally:

- A **systematic investigation** of phenomena by gathering **quantifiable data** and applying statistical, mathematical, or computational techniques.
- Deals with **numbers** and anything that is **measurable**.
- An entire quantitative study typically ends with **confirmation or disconfirmation** of a hypothesis.
- Researchers identify **one or a few variables** and collect data specifically related to those variables.

### Three Core Characteristics

| Characteristic | Description |
|---|---|
| **Data gathering instrument** | Experiments, surveys, measurements, equipment → produces numerical/measurable data |
| **Type of data** | Tables of numbers and statistics |
| **Approach** | Researcher remains **objectively separated** from the subject matter — seeks precise measurements only |

> **Key insight:** Quantitative research is *objective* — it does not interpret or assign meaning. It measures and analyses.
> 
> *Nghiên cứu định lượng là khách quan — không diễn giải hay gán ý nghĩa. Nó đo lường và phân tích.*

## 2. Quantitative vs Qualitative Research

| Dimension | Quantitative | Qualitative |
|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Numbers, measurements | Meaning, experiences |
| **Data type** | Numerical | Textual, observational |
| **Purpose** | Test hypotheses | Explore phenomena |
| **Questions** | How many? How much? | Why? How? |
| **Tools** | Surveys, experiments, statistics | Interviews, observation, coding |
| **Output** | Confirmed/rejected hypothesis | Themes, patterns, theory |
| **When to use** | End of design — measure improvement | Beginning of design — uncover innovations |

> **Professor's note:** These two approaches are complementary, not competing. Qualitative research often generates the hypotheses; quantitative research then tests them.
>
> *Hai phương pháp này bổ sung cho nhau. Định tính tạo ra giả thuyết; định lượng kiểm tra chúng.*

## 3. Three Levels of Quantitative Analysis

### 3.1 Descriptive
**"What exists?"** *"Cái gì đang tồn tại?"*

- Describes and documents phenomena as they naturally occur
- No experimental manipulation, no random assignment
- Uses: questionnaires, closed interviews, observation checklists
- Can: identify characteristics, explore correlations between entities
- Acts as a **springboard** for higher levels of analysis

**Example:** Counting how many students in a class use laptops vs tablets.

### 3.2 Explanatory
**"Why does it happen?"** *"Tại sao điều đó xảy ra?"*

- Goes beyond description to understand *causes and reasons*
- Uses statistical models to test explanations
- Asks: how reliable is the explanation? Are there alternative explanations?

**Example:** Why has the unemployment rate risen? Is it due to young people entering the market, or machines replacing workers?

### 3.3 Predictive
**"What will happen next?"** *"Điều gì sẽ xảy ra tiếp theo?"*

- Builds models of future outcomes
- Tests how well those models work
- Difficult at the individual level, easier at the group level

**Example:** Can we predict which students will receive university admission offers based on their school exam performance — before their public exam results are released?

## 4. Research Methods Under Descriptive Research

### 4.1 Observation Studies

- Used in both quantitative and qualitative research
- In quantitative: focus on a **particular, measurable factor** of behaviour
- The behaviour is **quantified** (counted, rated, timed)

**Common strategies:**

| Strategy | Description |
|---|---|
| Structured observation | Pre-defined categories, standardised recording |
| Time sampling | Record behaviour at fixed time intervals |
| Event sampling | Record every time a specific event occurs |

---

### 4.2 Correlational Research

**Definition:** Systematically investigates the **nature of relationships** between two or more variables in the real world.

> ⚠️ **Critical point:** Correlational research identifies and describes relationships. It does **NOT** establish causation — it cannot tell you that one variable *causes* the other.
>
> *Nghiên cứu tương quan mô tả mối quan hệ. Nó KHÔNG thể thiết lập mối quan hệ nhân quả.*

**Key features:**
- A correlation exists when one variable increases or decreases correspondingly with another
- Results are often represented using a **scatterplot**
- The strength and direction of a relationship is measured using a **correlation coefficient (r)**

#### Correlation Coefficient (r)
- Range: **-1 to +1**
- **r = +1**: perfect positive correlation (both variables increase together)
- **r = -1**: perfect negative correlation (one increases, the other decreases)
- **r = 0**: no linear correlation
- **|r| > 0.7**: strong | **0.3 < |r| < 0.7**: moderate | **|r| < 0.3**: weak

#### Steps to Draw a Scatterplot
1. **Decide the two variables** (e.g. hours of study vs exam score)
2. **Collect data** for both variables from the same subjects
3. **Map the data** — plot each paired observation as a point (x, y)
4. **Draw the line of best fit** (also called regression line or trend line)
5. **Interpret** — positive slope = positive correlation; negative slope = negative correlation; no clear pattern = no correlation

**Examples of correlational research:**
- Storage size and database capacity (as storage grows → easier to manage databases)
- Stress and depression levels
- Fame and income

---

### 4.3 Survey Research

**Definition (Kerlinger 1973):** A study of large and small populations by selecting samples to discover relative incidence, distribution, and interrelations.

**Ultimate goal:** Learn about a large population by surveying a representative sample.

**Common formats:** Face-to-face interviews, telephone interviews, questionnaires.

#### Two Types of Surveys

##### Cross-sectional Survey
*Khảo sát cắt ngang*

- Collects data from a sample at **one single point in time**
- Gives a "snapshot" of the population at that moment
- **Advantage:** Easier and cheaper to implement
- **Limitation:** Limited explanatory power — cannot capture change over time

**Example:** Administering a questionnaire to 500 UOW students about broadband usage — all at the same time.

##### Longitudinal Survey
*Khảo sát dọc*

- Data collected at **multiple points in time** to observe changes
- **Advantage:** Captures trends, tendencies, and opinions over time
- **Limitation:** Costly, difficult to administer, time-consuming

Two sub-types:

| Sub-type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Cohort study** | Identify a category, randomly select individuals, survey over time (sample may vary each time) | Research a population and at each data collection point, select a new sample to see trends |
| **Panel study** | Follow the **same individuals** across all data collection points | Track 100 specific people for 10 years, observing why they change |

**Example:** A survey studying the effects of exercise on 100 kids from age 12 to age 22 (10-year panel study).

#### Cross-sectional vs Longitudinal — Which to Choose?

| | Cross-sectional | Longitudinal |
|---|---|---|
| **Time** | One point | Multiple points over time |
| **Cost** | Low | High |
| **Information** | Snapshot only | Trends and change over time |
| **Difficulty** | Easy | Complex |
| **Use when** | Quick descriptive overview needed | Change over time is the research focus |

## 5. Basic Steps of Quantitative Research

Quantitative research uses **deductive reasoning**:
*Theory → Hypothesis → Data → Test → Conclusion*

*Nghiên cứu định lượng dùng suy luận diễn dịch: Lý thuyết → Giả thuyết → Dữ liệu → Kiểm tra → Kết luận.*

### The 5-Step Deductive Process

```
Step 1: Observe & Investigate
        ↓
Step 2: Form a Hypothesis
        ↓
Step 3: Predict Outcomes & Plan Data Collection
        ↓
Step 4: Collect & Process Data
        ├── Prediction CORRECT → Step 5
        └── Prediction WRONG → Return to Step 2
        ↓
Step 5: Verify Findings → State Final Conclusions
```

### Key Statistical Elements in Quantitative Research

| Element | Question it answers | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Counting** | How many X in our sample? | How many boys? |
| **Hypothesis testing** | Are there more X than Y in the population? | More boys than girls? |
| **Sample size & power** | How big does my sample need to be? | Need n = 100 to detect effect? |
| **Modelling variability** | How much do A & B determine C? | How much do age & gender determine academic results? |
| **Prediction** | Can I predict outcome from known variables? | Will a 16-year-old from Band 1 school pass? |

## 6. Hypothesis Testing — Core Theory

### What is a Statistical Hypothesis?

A **statistical hypothesis** is an assumption about a **population parameter**. This assumption may or may not be true.

*Giả thuyết thống kê là một giả định về tham số của tổng thể. Giả định này có thể đúng hoặc sai.*

Since we cannot study the entire population, we:
1. Take a **random sample**
2. Test whether the sample data is **consistent with the hypothesis**
3. If not consistent → **reject** the hypothesis

### Two Types of Hypotheses

#### H₀ — Null Hypothesis (Giả thuyết không)
- The hypothesis we initially **assume to be true**
- Researchers work to **reject** or **nullify** it
- **Always contains an equality sign: =, ≤, ≥**
- Represents: "no effect", "no difference", "status quo"

#### H₁ (or Hₐ) — Alternative Hypothesis (Giả thuyết thay thế)
- Set up in **opposition** to the null hypothesis
- What the researcher suspects or wants to prove
- **Never contains an equality sign: ≠, <, >**
- Represents: "there IS an effect", "there IS a difference"

### Classic Example — The Coin Flip

> **Research question:** Is this coin fair and balanced?

**Setting up hypotheses:**
```
H₀: p = 0.50    (the coin IS fair — Heads appears 50% of the time)
Hₐ: p ≠ 0.50   (the coin is NOT fair — proportion of Heads differs from 50%)
```

**Experiment:** Flip the coin 50 times → 40 Heads, 10 Tails.

**Reasoning:**
- If the coin were fair, we'd expect approximately 25 Heads and 25 Tails
- Getting 40 Heads out of 50 is very unlikely if p = 0.50
- The evidence is **inconsistent** with H₀
- **Decision: Reject H₀** → The coin is probably not fair

> **Professor's note:** We never "prove" H₀ true. We either REJECT H₀ (evidence is inconsistent) or FAIL TO REJECT H₀ (evidence is consistent — but this doesn't mean H₀ is definitely true).
>
> *Ta không bao giờ "chứng minh" H₀ đúng. Ta chỉ bác bỏ nó hoặc không bác bỏ nó.*

### Three Types of Tests (Direction of H₁)

| Test type | H₁ | When to use | Rejection region |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Two-tailed** | p ≠ p₀ | Research question asks if value is "different from" | Both tails |
| **Left-tailed** | p < p₀ | Research question asks if value is "less than" | Left tail only |
| **Right-tailed** | p > p₀ | Research question asks if value is "greater than" | Right tail only |

> **How to identify which test:** Look at the research question's wording.
> - "Different from" or "not equal to" → **two-tailed**
> - "Less than" or "decreased" → **left-tailed**  
> - "Greater than" or "increased" → **right-tailed**

## 7. The Bernoulli Distribution & Standard Error

### Bernoulli Distribution

A **Bernoulli distribution** applies to variables with only **two possible outcomes** (Boolean/binary):
- Outcome = 1 with probability **p** (success)
- Outcome = 0 with probability **1 - p = q** (failure)

*Phân phối Bernoulli áp dụng cho biến chỉ có hai kết quả có thể: thành công (1) hoặc thất bại (0).*

**Examples:** Coin flip (Heads/Tails), Test result (Pass/Fail), Survey response (Yes/No)

### Estimating the Population Proportion p

When we take a sample of size **n** from a Bernoulli population, we estimate p using:

```
         number of "successes" in sample
p̂  =  ─────────────────────────────────
                  n
```

Where p̂ (read: "p-hat") is the **sample proportion**.

**Example:** 6 out of 40 UOW graduating students plan to go to graduate school.
```
p̂ = 6/40 = 0.15
```
Our estimate is that **15%** of graduating students plan to go to graduate school.

### The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

> **Central Limit Theorem:** When independent random variables are added together, their sum (properly normalised) tends toward a **Normal distribution** — even if the original variables are not normally distributed — as sample size increases.
>
> *Khi cộng các biến ngẫu nhiên độc lập lại, tổng chuẩn hoá của chúng có xu hướng tiến về phân phối chuẩn khi cỡ mẫu tăng lên.*

**Why this matters:** The CLT allows us to use the properties of the Normal distribution to make inferences about ANY population, regardless of its distribution, as long as n is large enough (typically n ≥ 30).

### Standard Error of the Sample Proportion

Because p̂ is calculated from a sample, it is not exactly equal to the true p. The **standard error (SE)** tells us how much our p̂ is expected to "miss" the true p on average.

```
         √(p̂ × (1 - p̂))
SE(p̂) = ─────────────────
               √n
```

*Sai số chuẩn cho biết p̂ trung bình "lệch" khỏi p thực sự bao nhiêu.*

**Continuing the example:**
```
p̂ = 0.15,  n = 40

SE = √(0.15 × 0.85) / √40
   = √(0.1275) / √40
   = 0.357 / 6.32
   = 0.0565  ≈ 5.65%
```
Our estimate of 15% has a standard error of about 5.65%.

### The Standardised Test Statistic (z-score)

Using the CLT, for large samples, the following quantity follows an approximately **standard normal distribution N(0,1)**:

```
         p̂ - p₀
z  =  ─────────────
         SE(p̂)
```

Where:
- **p̂** = observed sample proportion
- **p₀** = hypothesised population proportion (from H₀)
- **SE(p̂)** = standard error (using p₀ in the denominator for hypothesis testing)

> **Note for hypothesis testing:** When computing z, we use **p₀** (not p̂) in the standard error formula, because under H₀ we assume p = p₀ is true.

```
              p̂ - p₀
z  =  ─────────────────────
         √(p₀ × (1 - p₀) / n)
```

## 8. Five-Step Hypothesis Testing Procedure

### Overview of the 5 Steps

```
Step 1 → Write H₀ and H₁
Step 2 → Calculate the test statistic
Step 3 → Find the p-value
Step 4 → Compare p-value to α → Make decision
Step 5 → Write a real-world conclusion
```

---

### Step 1: State the Hypotheses

- H₀ always contains **=** (equality)
- H₁ never contains equality: uses **≠**, **<**, or **>**
- p₀ is the **hypothesised value** of the population proportion

**Template:**
```
H₀: p = p₀        (no difference from hypothesised value)
H₁: p ≠ p₀        (two-tailed)
  OR p < p₀        (left-tailed)
  OR p > p₀        (right-tailed)
```

---

### Step 2: Calculate the Test Statistic

For a proportion test (z-test):

```
              p̂ - p₀
z  =  ─────────────────────
         √(p₀(1 - p₀) / n)
```

**Interpretation of z:**
- This finds the **z-score** for our observed sample proportion, assuming H₀ is true
- A large |z| means our sample is far from what H₀ predicts → evidence against H₀
- A small |z| means our sample is close to what H₀ predicts → consistent with H₀

---

### Step 3: Find the P-value

The **p-value** is the probability of obtaining a sample result **as extreme as or more extreme** than what we observed, *assuming H₀ is true*.

*P-value là xác suất thu được kết quả mẫu cực đoan ít nhất bằng kết quả quan sát được, giả sử H₀ đúng.*

| Test type | p-value formula |
|---|---|
| Two-tailed | p-value = 2 × P(Z < -|z|) |
| Left-tailed | p-value = P(Z < z) |
| Right-tailed | p-value = P(Z > z) |

> **Critical understanding:** A **small p-value** means it would be very unlikely to see our data if H₀ were true — so H₀ is probably false.
>
> *P-value nhỏ → dữ liệu rất khó xảy ra nếu H₀ đúng → H₀ có thể sai.*

---

### Step 4: Make the Decision

Compare p-value to the **significance level α** (usually α = 0.05):

```
If p-value ≤ α  →  REJECT H₀     (results are statistically significant)
If p-value > α  →  FAIL TO REJECT H₀  (insufficient evidence to reject)
```

| α = 0.05 means... | We accept a 5% risk of incorrectly rejecting H₀ |
|---|---|
| **Statistically significant** | Results are unlikely to be due to chance alone |
| **Not significant** | Results could plausibly occur by chance |

> ⚠️ We say **"fail to reject H₀"** — NOT "accept H₀". Not rejecting doesn't mean it's true; it means we don't have enough evidence to reject it.
>
> *Ta nói "không bác bỏ H₀" — không phải "chấp nhận H₀". Không bác bỏ không có nghĩa là đúng.*

---

### Step 5: State a Real-World Conclusion

Write a plain-language conclusion that directly addresses the original research question — not just statistical language.

**Template:**
- If reject H₀: *"We have sufficient evidence to conclude that [alternative hypothesis in plain words]."*
- If fail to reject H₀: *"We do not have sufficient evidence to conclude that [alternative hypothesis in plain words]."*

---

### Complete Worked Example — Babies

**Research question:** Is the proportion of babies born male different from 0.50?

**Data:** n = 200 babies; 97 were male → p̂ = 97/200 = 0.485

#### Step 1: Hypotheses
```
H₀: p = 0.50      (proportion of males IS 50%)
Hₐ: p ≠ 0.50      (proportion of males is NOT 50%)
→ TWO-TAILED test (research asks "different from", not "more" or "less")
```

#### Step 2: Calculate z
```
         p̂ - p₀               0.485 - 0.50
z = ─────────────────  =  ───────────────────────
     √(p₀(1-p₀)/n)        √(0.50 × 0.50 / 200)

         -0.015              -0.015
  =  ──────────────  =  ─────────────  =  -0.566
     √(0.0025/1)          0.03536
                        √(0.001250)
```
Wait, let me redo:
```
√(p₀(1-p₀)/n) = √(0.50 × 0.50 / 200) = √(0.25/200) = √(0.00125) = 0.03536

z = (0.485 - 0.50) / 0.03536 = -0.015 / 0.03536 = -0.424
```

*(The lecture states z = -0.566; exact value depends on precise count of males)*

#### Step 3: Find p-value (Two-tailed)
```
p-value = 2 × P(Z < -0.566)
        = 2 × 0.2843
        = 0.5686
```
The probability of getting a result this extreme (or more extreme), if H₀ is true, is **56.86%**.

```
p-value = 0.5686 > α = 0.05

```

> "We do not have sufficient evidence to state that the proportion of babies born male in the population is different from 0.50."

## 9. Z-Test vs T-Test — When to Use Which

| | Z-Test | T-Test |
|---|---|---|
| **Population variance** | **Known** | **Unknown** (estimated from sample) |
| **Sample size** | Typically large (n > 30) | Any size, but especially useful for small samples |
| **Distribution used** | Standard Normal Z ~ N(0,1) | Student's t-distribution with (n-1) degrees of freedom |
| **Key formula difference** | Uses σ (true standard deviation) | Uses s (sample standard deviation) |

*Dùng Z-test khi biết phương sai tổng thể; dùng T-test khi không biết (phổ biến hơn trong thực tế).*

> **In practice:** The population variance is almost never known. Therefore, the **t-test is used far more often** than the z-test in real research.

### Why Degrees of Freedom?
*Tại sao lại dùng bậc tự do?*

When we estimate the population variance using the sample (s²), we "use up" one observation to calculate the mean first. So we have:

```
Degrees of freedom (df) = n - 1
```

The t-distribution with lower df has heavier tails than the Normal distribution — reflecting the additional uncertainty from estimating σ.

## 10. One-Sample T-Test

### When to Use
- You have **one sample** of data
- You want to test whether the **population mean** equals a specific hypothesised value
- Population variance is **unknown**

*Khi bạn có một mẫu và muốn kiểm tra xem trung bình tổng thể có bằng một giá trị cụ thể không.*

### Hypotheses

```
H₀: μ = μ₀        (population mean equals the hypothesised value)
H₁: μ ≠ μ₀        (two-tailed)
  OR μ < μ₀        (left-tailed)
  OR μ > μ₀        (right-tailed)
```

### Test Statistic Formula

```
         x̄ - μ₀
t   =   ─────────
          s / √n
```

Where:
- **x̄** = sample mean
- **μ₀** = hypothesised population mean (from H₀)
- **s** = sample standard deviation
- **n** = sample size
- **s / √n** = standard error of the mean (SE)

### Steps

1. Compute **x̄** (sample mean) and **s** (sample standard deviation)
2. Plug into the t formula above
3. Determine **df = n - 1**
4. Find the p-value by comparing t to the t-distribution with (n-1) df:
   - Two-tailed: `p = 2 × P(T > |t|)`
   - Upper-tailed: `p = P(T > t)`
   - Lower-tailed: `p = P(T < t)`
5. Compare p to α; decide to reject or fail to reject H₀

### Worked Example — Laptop Weights

**Research question:** Does the assembly line produce laptops that weigh exactly 2 kg?

**Data:** Sample of n = 15 laptops.
- Sample mean: x̄ = 2.12 kg
- Sample SD: s = 0.18 kg
- Hypothesised mean: μ₀ = 2.00 kg

#### Step 1: Hypotheses
```
H₀: μ = 2.00 kg    (mean weight IS 2 kg)
H₁: μ ≠ 2.00 kg    (mean weight is NOT 2 kg)
→ Two-tailed test
```

#### Step 2: Calculate t
```
         x̄ - μ₀         2.12 - 2.00        0.12
t  =  ───────────  =  ──────────────  =  ────────  =  2.582
         s / √n         0.18 / √15        0.0465
```

#### Step 3: Find p-value
```
df = n - 1 = 15 - 1 = 14

For t = 2.582 with df = 14 (two-tailed):
p-value = 2 × P(T₁₄ > 2.582) ≈ 0.021
```

#### Step 4: Decision
```
p-value = 0.021 < α = 0.05

→ REJECT H₀
```

#### Step 5: Conclusion
> "We have sufficient evidence to conclude that the assembly line does NOT produce laptops with a mean weight of 2 kg. The sample suggests the true mean weight is significantly different from 2 kg."

## 11. Two-Sample T-Test

### When to Use
- You have **two independent groups/samples**
- You want to test whether the **population means of the two groups are equal**
- Most common application: testing whether a new treatment/process is better than the current one

*Khi bạn có hai nhóm độc lập và muốn kiểm tra xem trung bình của chúng có bằng nhau không.*

### Key Design Decisions

| Decision | Option A | Option B |
|---|---|---|
| **Data structure** | **Paired** (one-to-one correspondence between samples) | **Unpaired/Independent** (no correspondence) |
| **Variance assumption** | **Equal variances** (simpler formulas) | **Unequal variances** (Welch's t-test) |
| **Null hypothesis** | μ₁ - μ₂ = 0 (means are equal) | μ₁ - μ₂ = d₀ (difference equals threshold d₀) |

### Hypotheses (Equal Means)

```
H₀: μ₁ = μ₂       (or equivalently: μ₁ - μ₂ = 0)
H₁: μ₁ ≠ μ₂       (two-tailed)
  OR μ₁ < μ₂       (left-tailed)
  OR μ₁ > μ₂       (right-tailed)
```

### Test Statistic (Assuming Equal Variances)

```
             x̄₁ - x̄₂
t  =   ─────────────────────────
        Sp × √(1/n₁ + 1/n₂)
```

Where **Sp** is the **pooled standard deviation**:

```
        √[ (n₁-1)s₁² + (n₂-1)s₂² ]
Sp  =   ─────────────────────────────
                n₁ + n₂ - 2
```

And degrees of freedom:
```
df = n₁ + n₂ - 2
```

### Worked Example — US vs Japanese Cars (MPG)

**Research question:** Is the mean fuel efficiency (miles per gallon) of US cars different from Japanese cars?

**Summary statistics:**
- Sample 1 (US cars): n₁ = 249, x̄₁ = 20.14 mpg, s₁ = 6.41
- Sample 2 (Japanese cars): n₂ = 79, x̄₂ = 30.48 mpg, s₂ = 6.11

#### Step 1: Hypotheses
```
H₀: μ₁ = μ₂      (US and Japanese cars have equal mean MPG)
H₁: μ₁ ≠ μ₂      (means are different)
→ Two-tailed test
```

#### Step 2: Calculate pooled SD and t
```
Sp = √[ (249-1)(6.41²) + (79-1)(6.11²) ] / (249 + 79 - 2)

   = √[ (248)(41.09) + (78)(37.33) ] / 326

   = √[ 10,190.32 + 2,911.74 ] / 326

   = √[ 13,102.06 / 326 ]

   = √40.19  =  6.34
```

```
t = (20.14 - 30.48) / (6.34 × √(1/249 + 1/79))

  = -10.34 / (6.34 × √(0.00402 + 0.01266))

  = -10.34 / (6.34 × √0.01668)

  = -10.34 / (6.34 × 0.1292)

  = -10.34 / 0.819

  = -12.62
```

The lecture states: **|t| = 12.62** ✓

#### Step 3: Find Critical Value and p-value
```
df = n₁ + n₂ - 2 = 249 + 79 - 2 = 326
α = 0.05 (two-tailed)

Critical value: t₁₋α/₂, df  = t₀.₉₇₅, ₃₂₆  ≈  1.9673
```

#### Step 4: Decision
```
|t| = 12.62 > critical value = 1.9673

→ REJECT H₀   (our test statistic falls in the rejection region)
```

#### Step 5: Conclusion
> "We have sufficient evidence to conclude that the mean fuel efficiency of US cars is significantly different from Japanese cars at the 0.05 significance level."

*(Japanese cars are significantly more fuel efficient.)*

## 12. Paired T-Test

### When to Use
- The same subjects (or matched pairs) are measured **twice** — before and after
- There is a **one-to-one correspondence** between the two samples
- You want to test whether the **mean difference** between pairs is significantly different from zero

*Khi đo cùng đối tượng hai lần (trước và sau), hoặc hai đối tượng được ghép cặp.*

> **Why paired is more powerful:** A regular two-sample t-test includes variance from random differences between individuals. A paired test removes this by analysing the within-subject *differences* — which are much less variable.
>
> *Paired t-test mạnh hơn vì nó loại bỏ sự biến thiên giữa các cá nhân.*

### How It Works

**Key idea:** Instead of comparing two sets of raw scores, compute the **difference** for each pair, then run a one-sample t-test on those differences.

```
Step 1: Calculate dᵢ = X₁ᵢ - X₂ᵢ   for each pair i
Step 2: Calculate d̄ = mean of all differences
Step 3: Calculate sₐ = standard deviation of differences
Step 4: Run one-sample t-test: H₀: μd = 0
```

### Hypotheses

```
H₀: μd = 0        (mean difference between pairs IS zero — no effect)
H₁: μd ≠ 0        (two-tailed: there IS a significant difference)
  OR μd < 0        (left-tailed: "after" is smaller)
  OR μd > 0        (right-tailed: "after" is larger)
```

### Test Statistic

```
         d̄ - 0         d̄
t   =   ─────────  =  ──────
          sₐ/√n        sₐ/√n
```

Where:
- **d̄** = mean of all pairwise differences
- **sₐ** = standard deviation of the differences
- **n** = number of pairs
- **df = n - 1**

### Worked Example — Weight Loss Programme

**Research question:** Is the fitness facility's weight-loss programme effective?

**Data:** 8 participants measured before and after the programme (weights in kg):

| Person | Before (X₁) | After (X₂) | Difference d = X₁ - X₂ |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 85 | 82 | **3** |
| 2 | 90 | 85 | **5** |
| 3 | 78 | 76 | **2** |
| 4 | 95 | 88 | **7** |
| 5 | 70 | 70 | **0** |
| 6 | 88 | 83 | **5** |
| 7 | 82 | 79 | **3** |
| 8 | 76 | 73 | **3** |

#### Step 1: Hypotheses
```
H₀: μd = 0       (programme has NO effect — mean difference is zero)
H₁: μd > 0       (programme IS effective — participants weigh LESS after)
→ RIGHT-TAILED test (we expect weight to decrease, so d = before-after > 0)
```

#### Step 2: Calculate d̄ and sₐ
```
Differences: 3, 5, 2, 7, 0, 5, 3, 3

d̄ = (3+5+2+7+0+5+3+3) / 8 = 28/8 = 3.50 kg

For sₐ, first find each (dᵢ - d̄)²:
(3-3.5)² = 0.25
(5-3.5)² = 2.25
(2-3.5)² = 2.25
(7-3.5)² = 12.25
(0-3.5)² = 12.25
(5-3.5)² = 2.25
(3-3.5)² = 0.25
(3-3.5)² = 0.25

Sum = 32.00

sₐ = √(32.00 / (8-1)) = √(32/7) = √4.571 = 2.138
```

#### Step 3: Calculate t
```
         d̄         3.50          3.50
t   =  ──────  =  ──────────  =  ────────  =  4.63
        sₐ/√n      2.138/√8      0.7558
```

#### Step 4: Find p-value and Decision
```
df = n - 1 = 8 - 1 = 7
One-tailed (right-tail): p-value = P(T₇ > 4.63) ≈ 0.001

p-value = 0.001 < α = 0.05

→ REJECT H₀
```

#### Step 5: Conclusion
> "We have sufficient evidence to conclude that the weight-loss programme is effective. Participants lost a statistically significant mean of 3.5 kg after completing the programme (p < 0.05)."

## 13. Summary Cheat Sheet

### Choosing the Right Test

```
Is population variance known?
│
├── YES → Use Z-TEST
│         z = (x̄ - μ₀) / (σ/√n)
│
└── NO  → How many samples?
          │
          ├── ONE sample → ONE-SAMPLE T-TEST
          │   t = (x̄ - μ₀) / (s/√n),   df = n-1
          │
          └── TWO samples
                │
                ├── PAIRED (same subjects measured twice)
                │   → PAIRED T-TEST
                │   t = d̄ / (sₐ/√n),   df = n-1
                │
                └── INDEPENDENT (different groups)
                    → TWO-SAMPLE T-TEST
                    t = (x̄₁-x̄₂) / (Sp×√(1/n₁+1/n₂))
                    df = n₁+n₂-2
```

### Decision Rules Summary

| p-value vs α | Decision | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| p-value ≤ α | **Reject H₀** | Statistically significant |
| p-value > α | **Fail to reject H₀** | Not statistically significant |

### All Formulas at a Glance

| Formula | Symbol | Description |
|---|---|---|
| `p̂ = x/n` | Sample proportion | Estimated proportion from sample |
| `SE(p̂) = √(p̂(1-p̂)/n)` | Standard error | Expected deviation of p̂ from true p |
| `z = (p̂ - p₀) / √(p₀(1-p₀)/n)` | Z test statistic (proportion) | Standardised distance from H₀ |
| `t = (x̄ - μ₀) / (s/√n)` | t test statistic (one sample) | Standardised distance from μ₀ |
| `Sp = √[(n₁-1)s₁²+(n₂-1)s₂²/(n₁+n₂-2)]` | Pooled SD | Combined SD for two-sample t-test |
| `t = d̄ / (sₐ/√n)` | t test statistic (paired) | Test on mean of differences |

### Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **H₀ (Null Hypothesis)** | The assumption we wish to test; contains equality (=); represents "no effect" |
| **H₁ (Alternative Hypothesis)** | Opposition to H₀; contains ≠, <, or >; represents "there is an effect" |
| **α (Significance level)** | Threshold for decision-making; typically 0.05 (5% risk of false rejection) |
| **p-value** | Probability of observing data this extreme if H₀ is true |
| **Test statistic** | A number computed from sample data; measures how far sample is from H₀ |
| **Degrees of freedom (df)** | n-1 for one sample; n₁+n₂-2 for two independent samples |
| **Standard error** | How much sample statistic is expected to deviate from population parameter |
| **Statistically significant** | p-value ≤ α → result unlikely to be due to chance alone |
| **Two-tailed test** | Tests for difference in either direction (H₁: ≠) |
| **One-tailed test** | Tests for difference in a specific direction (H₁: < or >) |
| **Paired t-test** | Same subjects measured twice; analyses differences between pairs |
| **Pooled SD** | Combined estimate of SD from two independent groups |
