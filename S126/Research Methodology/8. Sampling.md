# Lecture 8 – Sampling

---

## 1. Key Concepts — Population & Sample

### Population (Tổng thể)
- A **population** is a collection of people, items, or events about which you want to make inferences
- **Universe**: total of all items or units in any field of inquiry
- **Population**: total of items about which information is desired
- The two terms are often used interchangeably

### Finite vs Infinite Population
| Type | Definition | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Finite** | Number of items is certain and countable | Citizens of a city, workers in a factory |
| **Infinite** | Number of items cannot be determined | Stars in the sky, listeners of a radio programme |

### Why Sample? (Tại sao cần lấy mẫu?)
- It is impossible to contact every member of a large population
- Example: A company wanting to study every person in a nation's attitudes towards a product — simply impossible!

---

## 2. What is Sampling?

- **Sampling** = Selection of a number of study units from a defined population
- A sample could be people, statistical variables, etc.

### 3 Questions in Sampling:
1. What is the set of elements from which we want to draw a sample?
2. How many elements do we need?
3. How will these elements be selected?

### Sample vs Population
| | Population | Sample |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope** | All elements in the data set | One or more observations from the population |
| **Measures** | Parameters | Statistics |
| **Feasibility** | Often impossible to study fully | Practical and manageable |

> A sample should be **randomly collected** and **adequately large** to represent the population well.

---

## 3. Sampling Frame (Khung lấy mẫu)

- A **sampling frame** = a list containing all sampling units from which the sample is to be drawn
- Can be constructed by the researcher or may consist of existing lists (e.g. telephone directory)
- Should be a **good representative** of the population
- If the population is finite and the time frame is present/past, the frame can be identical with the population

---

## 4. Statistic vs Parameter

| | Statistic | Parameter |
|---|---|---|
| **Source** | Characteristic of a **sample** | Characteristic of a **population** |
| **Example** | Sample mean, sample median | Population mean, population variance |
| **Purpose** | Used to estimate the parameter | The true value we want to know |

---

## 5. Sampling Error (Sai số lấy mẫu)

- Studying a small portion of the population naturally introduces inaccuracy
- This inaccuracy = **sampling error** or **error variance**
- Usually **random variations** in sample estimates around the true population values
- Larger sample sizes **reduce** sampling error (but at a decreasing rate)

---

## 6. Sample Design — Key Components

A sample design is a definite plan for obtaining a sample from a given population. Must be determined **before** data is collected.

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Sampling Unit** | The basic unit — can be geographical (state, district), structural (house, flat), social (family, school), or individual |
| **Sampling Frame** | The complete list of all sampling units |
| **Sample Size** | Should be optimum: not too large, not too small |
| **Sampling Technique** | The method used to select units |

### Optimum Sample Size fulfils:
- **Efficiency** — not wasteful of resources
- **Representativeness** — mirrors the population
- **Reliability** — consistent results
- **Flexibility** — adaptable to research needs

### Factors affecting sample size:
- Desired **precision**
- Acceptable **confidence level**
- **Population variance** — larger variance → larger sample needed

## 8. Probability Sampling (Lấy mẫu xác suất)

Also known as **random sampling** or **chance sampling**.

- Every item of the population has an **equal chance** of inclusion
- Uses a sampling frame + random number generation
- Results can be **measured in terms of probability**
- Ensures the **Law of Statistical Regularity** — sample will have the same composition and characteristics as the population
- Considered the **best technique** for representative samples
- Greatest freedom from bias — but **most costly** in time and energy

### 8a. Simple Random Sampling
- Every case has an **equal probability** of inclusion
- **Disadvantages:**
  - Requires a complete list of all units (full frame)
  - Costly if units are geographically scattered
  - Standard errors of estimators can be high

### 8b. Systematic Sampling
- Select items from an ordered population using a **skip interval**
- Formula: interval = population size ÷ sample size
- Example: select 1,000 from 50,000 → every 50th person (50,000/1,000 = 50)
- Better than random sampling when data does not exhibit patterns and there is low risk of data manipulation

### 8c. Stratified Random Sampling
- Population divided into **strata (subgroups)** → random sample taken from each subgroup
- Subgroups based on: company size, gender, occupation, etc.
- Used when there is **great variation** within a population
- Ensures every stratum is **adequately represented**

### 8d. Cluster Sampling
- Population divided into **clusters (groups)**
- A random sample of clusters is selected → all items within chosen clusters are used
- Best for populations that are **geographically fragmented**
- Saves time and money compared to simple random sampling

---

## 9. Non-probability Sampling (Lấy mẫu phi xác suất)

Also known as: deliberate sampling, purposive sampling, judgement sampling.

- No basis for estimating the probability of any item being included
- Items **deliberately selected** by researcher — researcher's judgment is supreme
- Risk: personal bias may vitiate (corrupt) the entire inquiry
- Rarely adopted in large, important inquiries
- Used in small inquiries where time and money are limited

### 9a. Quota Sampling
- Participants chosen based on **predetermined characteristics** so the sample has the same distribution as the wider population
- Interviewers given quotas to fill — actual selection left to interviewer's discretion

### 9b. Convenience Sampling
- Selecting participants because they are **readily and easily available**
- Inexpensive and easy — e.g. using friends or family
- Least rigorous method

### 9c. Snowball Sampling
- A few initial cases **encourage other cases** to participate — sample grows like a snowball
- Best for **small, hard-to-access populations** (e.g. secret societies, inaccessible professions)

---

## 10. Probability vs Non-probability — Comparison

| | Probability | Non-probability |
|---|---|---|
| **Selection** | Random — equal chance for all | Deliberate — researcher chooses |
| **Bias** | Minimal | High risk |
| **Representativeness** | High | Uncertain |
| **Cost** | High | Low |
| **Error measurement** | Can be measured | Cannot be measured |
| **Use case** | Large, important research | Small, exploratory research |

---

## 11. Determining Sample Size

- Size should be **optimum** — not too large, not too small
- What matters is **absolute size** relative to:
  - Complexity of the population
  - Aims of the researcher
- **Larger samples reduce sampling error** — but at a **decreasing rate**

### Key considerations:
1. **Desired precision** — how accurate does your estimate need to be?
2. **Confidence level** — how certain do you need to be? (e.g. 95%)
3. **Population variance** — more variance in population → larger sample needed

---

## 12. From Sample to Population — Key Principles

| Statement | Condition |
|---|---|
| Sample mean approaches population mean | As sample size increases (probability sample) |
| Sample variance is a good estimator of population variance | Sample size > 30 |
| Distribution of sample mean is approximately Normal | Sample size > 30 |
| Standard error = population SD ÷ √sample size | Always (for probability samples) |

### Three uses of sample mean:
1. **Point estimator** for the population mean
2. **Centre of a confidence interval** for the population mean
3. **Basis for hypothesis testing** of whether the population mean has a particular value

> Key insight: We use our knowledge of how the population relates to the sample to make **reverse statements** about the population from a selected sample.
