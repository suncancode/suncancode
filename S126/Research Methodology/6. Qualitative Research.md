# Lecture 6 – Qualitative Research

---

## 1. Overview

Qualitative research is an **approach for exploring and understanding a social or human problem** about individuals or groups.

### Key Characteristics
- Includes methods such as: **action research**, **case study research**, **grounded theory**, etc.
- Aims to answer questions about: **What? How? Why?**  
  *(contrast with quantitative research which asks "how many?" or "how much?")*

### The Research Process Involves:
1. **Emerging questions and procedures** — questions evolve as research progresses
2. **Data collected in the participant's setting** — naturalistic context
3. **Inductive data analysis** — building from specific particulars → general themes
4. **Researcher interpretation** — the researcher makes meaning from the data

### Output
- The final written report has a **flexible structure** (not rigid like quantitative reports)

---

## 2. Why Use Qualitative Research?

Other research methods (e.g., surveys, experiments) may be constrained by:

| Constraint | Description |
|---|---|
| Research conditions | Inability to establish necessary conditions (e.g., can't run an experiment) |
| Insufficient data | Not enough quantitative data available |
| Sampling problems | Difficulty drawing an adequate sample or achieving high response rate |
| Temporal limitations | Some methods only study past events, not ongoing phenomena |

### Practical Example
> Scenario: You want to lobby for better healthcare access where user fees exist.
> - A cross-sectional survey tells you **20% of the population lacks access** (quantitative)
> - But the survey can't tell you:
>   - *What are people's experiences of user fees?*
>   - *What other barriers exist to accessing healthcare?*
> - → Qualitative methods fill this gap and help **generate hypotheses** for later quantitative testing

---

## 3. Examples of Qualitative Research Methods

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| **Case Study** | Studies a phenomenon in its real-world context; investigates a contemporary phenomenon where boundaries between phenomenon and context are not clearly evident |
| **Action Research** | Researcher adopts an active role or collaborates with participants |
| **Grounded Theory** | Assumes social behavior in real-world contexts is best analyzed by deriving "bottom-up" grounded categories and concepts |

> ⚠️ *This lecture focuses in detail on **Grounded Theory***

### Case Study (More Detail)
- Examines a phenomenon in its **natural setting**
- Uses **multiple methods of data collection**
- Gathers information from **one or a few entities** (people, groups, organisations)

## 4. Six General Skills for Qualitative Researchers

| # | Skill | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Listening** | Ability to absorb large amounts of information about people and environments |
| 2 | **Asking good questions** | Accurate and continuous questioning to collect credible, critical information |
| 3 | **Knowing your topic** | Sufficient domain knowledge — understanding the problem, potential solutions, and expectations |
| 4 | **Caring about your data** | Carefully handle all data and documents |
| 5 | **Doing parallel tasks** | Work on multiple tasks simultaneously |
| 6 | **Persevering** | Be prepared for frustrations, uncertainties, and unpleasantries |

## 5. Ethical Challenges

| Issue | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Fair data examination** | What data to include? How much? How to handle unnecessary or negative instances? |
| **Codes of Ethics** | Provide guidance on maintaining research integrity |
| **Research Integrity** | Your work must be trusted as representing truthful positions and statements |
| **IRB Approval** | Obtain approval from an **Institutional Review Board** for all studies involving human participants |

## 6. Challenges of Starting Qualitative Research

Two core challenges:
- **Data collection**: Must collect your own original data
- **Originality**: Must produce your own ideas and words

### Four Essential Features to Consider:

| Feature | Key Question |
|---|---|
| **A Topic** | What are you going to study? |
| **Literature Review** | What are the existing research issues and methods? |
| **Data Collection Method** | How are you going to collect the data? |
| **Source of Data** | Where are you going to get the data? |

---

## 7. Formulating a Research Question

Ask yourself:
1. **Has this research already been done?** → Conduct a literature review
2. **Is this a problem that research can address?** → Be careful not to do research just to delay solving a problem where the answer is already known
3. **Is a qualitative approach appropriate?** → Yes, if you want to:
   - Understand participant perspectives
   - Explore the meaning they give to phenomena
   - Observe a process in depth

## 8. Research Proposal Structure

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Aims & Objectives** | The WHAT — broad aim + measurable objectives |
| **Background** | The WHY — why the question matters, what is already known |
| **Methods** | The HOW — setting, participants, recruitment, data collection, analysis plans |
| **Ethical Issues** | Whether ethical appraisal is needed and how you'll address ethical concerns |
| **Resources** | Costs (travel, staff, accommodation, stationery) and other resources (translation, analysis help) |
| **Time Scale** | How long; key milestones (data collection completion, analysis, report writing) |
| **Dissemination/Output** | Target audience and how conclusions will be shared (reports, conferences, workshops) |

## 9. Data Collection Methods

Four main methods:

| Method | Illustrative Types of Data | Specific Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Interviewing & Conversing** | Language — verbal and body | Explanation of behaviour; recollections |
| **Observing** | Gestures, social interactions, actions, physical environment | Coordination between people; spatial arrangements |
| **Collecting & Examining** | Personal documents, printed materials, graphics, archival records, physical artifacts | Titles, texts, dates, chronologies, written words |
| **Feeling** | Sensations | Coldness/warmth of a place; perceived time; others' comfort or discomfort |

---

### 9a. Interviewing — Key Types

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Structured Interview** | Formal questionnaire and standardized procedure |
| **Qualitative Interview** | No formal questionnaire — only a mental framework; adapts to each interviewee |
| **Group Interview** | Multiple participants at once |
| **Focus Group Interview** | Participants share common experience or views |

### 9b. Asking Good Questions — Guidelines

- **Start general** → orient the interview to the topic
- **Gauge language level** → avoid intimidating jargon OR being patronising
- **Use everyday vocabulary** → no overly technical or complicated words
- **Save sensitive questions for the end**
- **Ask open questions** → require more than "yes" or "no"
- **Ask neutral questions** → e.g., don't ask *"Why haven't you immunised your children?"* → ask *"How did you decide whether to immunise your children?"*
- **Use concrete rather than abstract questions** → e.g., *"Think about the last time you were pregnant. What did you like about services then?"* not *"What do you think about ante-natal services?"*
- **Use concrete events as anchors** → e.g., *"After your last child was born"* or *"The day of the earthquake"* rather than *"January 3rd"*

### 9c. Observing

- **Systematic observation**: Uses a formal instrument and a specific set of occasions
- Requires deciding:
  - **When and where** to observe
  - **What** to observe: individual people, interactions, actions, physical surroundings

### 9d. Collecting and Examining

- Collect objects related to your study topic
- Examine how collected material fits the rest of your study

### 9e. Feeling

- Use feelings to represent data about the environment (warmth, coldness, noisiness, quiet) and other people
- Record feelings and describe them as observable events, behaviours, or conditions

## 10. Analysing Qualitative Data — 5 Phases

```
1. Compile Database
       ↓
2. Disassemble Data  ←──────────────────┐
       ↓                               │
3. Reassemble Data                      │
       ↓                               │
4. Interpret Data ──────────────────────┘
       ↓
5. Conclude
```

> ⚠️ Note: The process is **recursive** — interpreting may send you back to disassembling, and so on.

---

### Phase 1 — Compiling

**Goal**: Organise data **systematically** before formal analysis begins

- Sort and compile field notes
- More orderly data → stronger analyses → more rigorous research
- The finished compilation is treated as a **database**

**Tools for compiling:**
- **Data dictionary**: defines each field and entry
- **Glossary**: defines key terminologies
- Put data into consistent form and separate into records
- Use **computer software** to manage records

---

### Phase 2 — Disassembling (= Open Coding / Level 1 Coding)

**Goal**: Break down compiled data into **smaller fragments or pieces**

- Assign new labels (**"codes"**) to fragments
- Codes can closely mirror the original language (even reuse exact words)
- Process may be repeated many times (trial and error) to test different codes

---

### Understanding Coding

**Coding** is the process of organising and sorting your data.

Codes serve to:
- Label, compile, and organise data
- Summarise and synthesise what is happening in your data
- Form the **basis for analysis** — *"coding IS analysis"*

#### Three Types of Coding (Grounded Theory Framework)

| Level | Name | Also Known As | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Level 1 | **Open Coding** | Disassembling | Break data into initial codes; stay close to original language |
| Level 2 | **Axial Coding** | Reassembling (step 1) | Relate categories to subcategories; link codes around an axis |
| Level 3 | **Selective Coding** | Reassembling (step 2) | Integrate and refine categories; identify the core/central category |

---

#### Open Coding (Level 1)

- Identify concepts and discover their properties and dimensions in the data
- Data broken down into discrete parts → compared for similarities and differences
- Theory building through **conceptualisation** (abstraction) and **comparative analysis**
- Concepts then categorised into **main- and subcategories**
- Proceeds to **Level 2 (category codes)** as patterns emerge

##### Example: Level 1 → Level 2 Coding

| Original Field Notes | Initial Code (Level 1) | Category Code (Level 2) |
|---|---|---|
| "Samantha brought homework home but didn't always have the right assignment" | Student oversight | Barrier for getting homework done |
| "Samantha's mother was usually busy when asked to help" | Parent unavailability | Barrier for getting homework done |
| "Mother was frequently interrupted and didn't return to help" | External interruption | Barrier for getting homework done |
| "Teacher reported Samantha seemed exceedingly talented" | Positive teacher's views | Positive expectations for homework |
| "Samantha expressed enjoyment in doing schoolwork" | Positive student's views | Positive expectations for homework |

---

#### Axial Coding (Level 2)

- Relates categories to their **subcategories**
- Called "axial" because coding occurs **around the axis** of the category
- Links categories at the level of their **properties and dimensions**
- Produces **more complete explanations** of the phenomenon

---

#### Selective Coding (Level 3)

- Integrates and **refines** the theory
- Recognises relationships between categories
- Identifies the **central / core category** — the main theme of the research
- Represents the analyst's interpretation of **what the research is fundamentally about**

---

                     Ground Theory

### Phase 3 — Reassembling

**Goal**: Reorganise disassembled pieces into **new groupings and sequences**

Questions to guide reassembling:
- Do the emerging patterns make sense?
- Are they moving you to a substantively important plane?
- How do patterns relate to original concepts and hypotheses?
- Do patterns grow more complex when you add more data?

**Techniques:**
- **Hierarchical arrays** — pull similar items together at each abstract level
- **Matrices** — rows represent one dimension, columns another (e.g., actions over time)
- **Multi-dimensional matrices** — for more complex arrangements

---

### Phase 4 — Interpreting

**Goal**: Make sense of the reassembled patterns and draw meaning

- Interpreting may **loop back** to disassembling (recoding) → which creates new themes in reassembling

**What constitutes a good interpretation?**

| Criterion | Question to Ask |
|---|---|
| **Completeness** | Does your interpretation have a beginning, middle, and end? |
| **Fairness** | Would others with the same interpretive stance arrive at the same conclusion? |
| **Empirical Accuracy** | Does your interpretation fairly represent your data? |
| **Value-Added** | Is this new knowledge, or just repetition of existing literature? |
| **Credibility** | How would esteemed peers in your field critique or accept this? |

Also:
- Explain how the outcome **solves the research problem**
- Explain why it is **novel**
- **Compare** your interpretation with other works

---

### Phase 5 — Concluding

**Goal**: Wrap up and point to the future

**Highlight key results:**
- Recall the research problem
- Emphasise novelty
- Emphasise significance

**Identify open problems:**
- Problems you could not solve in this research
- Suggest next steps / future research directions

---

## 11. Common Criticisms of Qualitative Research

| Criticism | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Small, unrepresentative samples** | Difficult to generalise findings to broader populations |
| **Lack of rigour** | Findings may be seen as subjective or anecdotal |
| **Researcher bias** | Hard to determine how much the findings reflect the researcher's own opinions vs reality |

      └── 5. Conclude → key results + open problems
