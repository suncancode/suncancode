## 1. What is Experimental Research?

### Definition

Experimental research is a **scientific approach** where:
- One or more **independent variables** are **manipulated** (changed on purpose)
- Their effect on one or more **dependent variables** is **measured**
- The researcher records changes over time to draw conclusions about cause-and-effect

*Nghiên cứu thực nghiệm là cách tiếp cận khoa học trong đó biến độc lập bị thao tác có chủ ý để đo tác động của nó lên biến phụ thuộc.*

### Key Features
- Most familiar form of research design
- Used in: physical sciences, social sciences, psychology, education
- Based on **comparison between two or more groups**
- Logic is straightforward, but execution can be difficult
- Often laboratory-based; usually involves quantitative data and statistical analysis

### Classic Example — The Plant Experiment

> Two plants of the same species receive identical treatment (same water, soil, care).
> - **Sample A**: placed in sunlight
> - **Sample B**: kept away from sunlight
>
> After the experiment: Sample A grows, Sample B dies.
>
> **Conclusion:** Sunlight causes growth in this type of plant.

| Variable | What it is in this example |
|---|---|
| **Independent variable** | Sunlight (manipulated — present or absent) |
| **Dependent variable** | Plant growth (what is measured) |
| **Extraneous variable** | Water, soil type, temperature (held constant) |

## 2. Key Variables

### Independent Variable (IV)
*Biến độc lập*

- The variable the researcher **deliberately changes or controls**
- The "cause" in a cause-and-effect relationship
- Different levels of the IV are called **conditions**
- Example: dosage of a drug (high dose / low dose / no dose)

### Dependent Variable (DV)
*Biến phụ thuộc*

- The variable the researcher **measures** to see if it changed
- The "effect" in a cause-and-effect relationship
- Its value *depends* on what happens to the IV
- Example: patient's health outcome after taking the drug

### Extraneous Variable
*Biến ngoại lai*

- Any variable **other than** the IV that could affect the DV
- The researcher must **control** these — either by holding them constant or measuring them
- If not controlled → they can "contaminate" results → you can't tell if DV changed because of IV or because of the extraneous variable
- Example: in a drug trial, age and gender of patients must be controlled

> **Professor's key insight:** The whole purpose of experimental design is to isolate the effect of the IV by eliminating or controlling all extraneous variables. If you can't do this cleanly, your conclusion is unreliable.
>
> *Mục đích của thiết kế thực nghiệm là cô lập tác động của biến độc lập bằng cách loại bỏ hoặc kiểm soát tất cả các biến ngoại lai.*

## 3. Conceptual vs Empirical Research

| | Conceptual Research | Empirical Research |
|---|---|---|
| **Based on** | Abstract ideas and theory | Observable evidence and experience |
| **Verification** | Logical reasoning | Direct observation or experiment |
| **Output** | Conceptual frameworks, theoretical models | Measurable, observable findings |
| **Example** | A philosopher theorising about justice | A study measuring recidivism rates after prison reform |

> **Note:** Experimental research is a form of *empirical* research — it requires direct observation and measurement, not just theoretical reasoning.

## 4. Experimental vs Non-experimental Hypothesis Testing

When research **tests a hypothesis**, it falls into one of two categories depending on whether the IV is manipulated:

### Experimental Hypothesis-testing Research
*Nghiên cứu kiểm định giả thuyết thực nghiệm*

- The **independent variable IS manipulated** by the researcher
- Can establish **causal relationships**
- Example: randomly assign 150 students into two groups:
  - Group A (control): parents have regular income
  - Group B (experimental): parents do not have regular income
  - Measure school attendance for both groups
  - → The IV (parental income stability) was manipulated by the researcher

### Non-experimental Hypothesis-testing Research
*Nghiên cứu kiểm định giả thuyết phi thực nghiệm*

- The **independent variable is NOT manipulated**
- Can only establish **correlational relationships**, not causation
- Example: calculate the correlation coefficient between family income and school attendance
  - → No one's income was changed — the researcher just measured what already existed

### Comparison Table

| | Experimental | Non-experimental |
|---|---|---|
| **IV manipulation** | Yes — researcher changes IV | No — researcher only observes |
| **What it can show** | Causation | Correlation only |
| **Example** | Drug trial with treatment/control | Survey correlating income and health |
| **Strength** | Strongest evidence for cause-and-effect | Cannot prove causation |

## 5. Experimental Group vs Control Group

### Definitions

| Group | Definition | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Control Group** | Exposed to **usual/normal conditions** — no new treatment | Provides the baseline for comparison |
| **Experimental Group** | Exposed to the **new or special condition** (the treatment) | Shows what happens when IV is applied |

### Rules
- If BOTH groups receive some special feature → both are called experimental groups
- A research design may include only experimental groups, or both experimental + control
- Random allocation to groups is essential

### Drug Trial Example (from lecture)

> Dementia patients randomly divided into 3 groups:
> - **Group 1** (experimental): High dosage of drug
> - **Group 2** (experimental): Low dosage of drug
> - **Group 3** (control): Placebo (sugar pill — looks like drug but has no effect)

*Why include a placebo group?* Because some people feel better simply from believing they are being treated — the "placebo effect." The control group isolates this psychological effect from the actual drug effect.

*Tại sao có nhóm giả dược? Vì một số người cảm thấy tốt hơn chỉ vì tin rằng họ đang được điều trị. Nhóm kiểm soát giúp tách biệt hiệu ứng này.*

## 6. Three Participant Allocation Designs

This answers the question: **"How do we assign participants to groups?"**

*Câu hỏi: Làm thế nào để phân bổ người tham gia vào các nhóm?*

---

### 6.1 Independent Measures Design
*(Also called: Between-groups design / Between-subjects design)*

**Definition:** Different participants are used in each condition. Each group = a different set of people.

*Mỗi nhóm gồm những người khác nhau hoàn toàn.*

**How it works:**
- Randomly allocate each participant to ONE condition only
- Each person only experiences one level of the IV

**Medicine example (10 participants):**
- 5 people → Group A (treatment)
- 5 different people → Group B (placebo)

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| No order effects (each person only does one thing) | Individual differences between groups may affect results (age, gender, background) |
| High quality results per condition | Needs more participants than repeated measures |

**Control:** Random allocation (every participant has equal chance of going to either group)

---

### 6.2 Repeated Measures Design
*(Also called: Within-groups design / Within-subjects design)*

**Definition:** The same participants take part in **all** conditions of the experiment.

*Cùng một người tham gia vào tất cả các điều kiện thử nghiệm.*

**How it works:**
- All 10 participants experience BOTH conditions (treatment and placebo)
- Measured in both conditions — usually at different times

**Medicine example (10 participants):**
- All 10 people → take treatment drug → measure → then take placebo → measure again
- (Or in opposite order for half the group)

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Eliminates individual differences between groups (same people in all conditions) | **Order effects**: performance in condition 2 may be influenced by condition 1 |
| Needs fewer participants | Takes more time |

**Control:** **Counterbalancing** — half the participants do condition A then B; the other half do condition B then A. This balances out order effects.

*Đối trọng hoá: chia đôi nhóm, nửa làm A→B, nửa làm B→A. Triệt tiêu hiệu ứng thứ tự.*

---

### 6.3 Matched Pairs Design

**Definition:** Participants are **matched in pairs** based on key variables (age, gender, IQ, medical history, etc.). One member of each pair goes to experimental group, the other goes to control group.

*Ghép cặp người tham gia theo các biến quan trọng. Một người mỗi cặp vào nhóm thực nghiệm, người còn lại vào nhóm kiểm soát.*

**How it works:**
- Find pairs of people who are very similar
- Randomly assign one from each pair to each condition

**Medicine example:** Two groups matched for age, gender, medical history.

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Reduces participant variables (matched pairs are similar) | Very hard to find perfectly matched pairs |
| Can isolate the effect of a single variable | If one participant drops out, you lose BOTH members of the pair |
| Avoids order effects | Matching can never be perfect — unknown differences remain |

---

### Comparison Table — Three Designs

| Feature | Independent Measures | Repeated Measures | Matched Pairs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Same or different people?** | Different people per condition | Same people in all conditions | Matched pairs — one per condition |
| **Individual differences** | High risk | Eliminated | Reduced |
| **Order effects** | None | Risk — use counterbalancing | None |
| **Participants needed** | Most | Fewest | Medium |
| **Main weakness** | Participant variability | Order effects | Hard to match perfectly |

## 7. Three Principles of Experimental Design

These are the **foundational principles** that all good experimental designs must follow.

*Ba nguyên tắc nền tảng mà mọi thiết kế thực nghiệm tốt đều phải tuân theo.*

### Principle 1: Replication
*Nguyên tắc lặp lại*

**What it means:** Each treatment is applied to **multiple** experimental units — not just one. The experiment is repeated more than once.

**Why it matters:**
- Results from a single measurement could be a fluke
- Multiple measurements give a more reliable, accurate estimate
- Reduces the experimental error
- Provides a more precise estimate of the mean effect of a treatment

**Example:**
> Testing two varieties of rice on a field. 
> - Without replication: divide field in half, grow each variety on one half → could be affected by unequal soil fertility in the two halves.
> - With replication: divide field into many parts, alternate which variety grows in each part → soil variation averages out, results are more reliable.

---

### Principle 2: Randomisation
*Nguyên tắc ngẫu nhiên*

**What it means:** Treatments are assigned to experimental units through a **random process** — every possible assignment has equal probability.

**Why it matters:**
- Protects against bias and extraneous variation that cannot be controlled
- Ensures no systematic advantage to any particular group
- Forms the basis for valid statistical testing

**Example:**
> Without randomisation: growing one rice variety in the first half of the field and the other variety in the second half — but the first half may have better soil. The result reflects soil quality, not the variety.
>
> With randomisation: randomly assign which variety goes in which plot → soil quality effects are distributed randomly across both varieties → fair comparison.

---

### Principle 3: Local Control
*Nguyên tắc kiểm soát cục bộ*

**What it means:** Designing the experiment so that **known extraneous sources of variation are brought under control** — measured, managed, and eliminated from the error term.

**Why it matters:**
- Increases efficiency by decreasing experimental error
- Makes the test more sensitive to the actual treatment effect
- The extraneous factor is made to vary deliberately across blocks, so its variability can be measured and subtracted out

**How it works in practice:**
- Divide experimental units into **blocks** where units within each block are homogeneous
- Then apply all treatments within each block
- Variation between blocks is measured and removed → only within-block variation remains as error

**Example:**
> Testing fertilisers on a field with varying soil fertility.
> - Divide the field into blocks based on soil fertility (each block has uniform soil)
> - Apply all fertiliser types within each block
> - Results: differences due to soil fertility are captured in the block effect and removed, leaving only the fertiliser effect

## 8. Informal Experimental Designs

These use **less sophisticated analysis** — based on observed differences in magnitude, without complex statistical procedures.

*Thiết kế không chính thức: phân tích đơn giản hơn, dựa trên sự khác biệt về độ lớn.*

### 8.1 Before-and-After WITHOUT Control Design
*(Thiết kế trước-sau, không có nhóm kiểm soát)*

**Structure:**
1. Select ONE group
2. Measure DV **before** treatment (baseline)
3. Introduce the treatment
4. Measure DV **again after** treatment
5. Compare the two measurements

```
One group:  [Measure] → [Treatment] → [Measure]
```

**Strength:** Simple — only one group needed.

**Weakness:** Time passes between measurements → extraneous variables may change. You don't know if the DV changed because of the treatment OR because of something else that happened over time.

*Điểm yếu: Thời gian trôi qua → các biến ngoại lai có thể thay đổi → không rõ DV thay đổi do điều trị hay do yếu tố khác.*

---

### 8.2 After-Only WITH Control Design
*(Thiết kế sau-chỉ, có nhóm kiểm soát)*

**Structure:**
1. Select TWO groups: test area and control area
2. Introduce treatment into the **test area only**
3. Measure DV in **both** areas at the **same time** after treatment

```
Test area:     [Treatment] → [Measure]
Control area:  [No treatment] → [Measure]
Compare the two measurements.
```

**Strength:** No time-lapse problem — both measured at the same time.

**Weakness:** Assumes the two areas are **identical** before the experiment. If they're not, extraneous variation could enter. There's no way to verify this because no baseline measurement was taken.

*Điểm yếu: Giả định hai nhóm giống hệt nhau trước thực nghiệm — không có cách kiểm tra vì không có đo lường trước.*

---

### 8.3 Before-and-After WITH Control Design
*(Thiết kế trước-sau, có nhóm kiểm soát)*

**Structure:**
1. Select TWO groups: test area and control area
2. Measure DV in **both** groups **before** treatment (establish baseline)
3. Introduce treatment into **test area only**
4. Measure DV in **both** groups **after** treatment

```
Test area:     [Measure] → [Treatment] → [Measure]
Control area:  [Measure] → [No treatment] → [Measure]
Compare: (Test after − Test before) vs (Control after − Control before)
```

**Strength:** The BEST of the three informal designs.
- Avoids extraneous variation from the passage of time (because control group also experiences time)
- Avoids the problem of non-comparable groups (because you have baseline measurements)

*Thiết kế tốt nhất trong ba loại không chính thức vì kiểm soát cả thời gian và sự không tương đương ban đầu.*

### Comparison — Three Informal Designs

| Design | Groups | Before measure? | After measure? | Controls time? | Controls group difference? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Before-after, no control | 1 | Yes | Yes | No | N/A |
| After-only, with control | 2 | No | Yes | Yes | No |
| Before-after, with control | 2 | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |

> **Verdict:** Before-and-after with control design is the most rigorous of the three.

## 9. Formal Experimental Designs

These use **precise statistical procedures** and offer more control than informal designs.

*Thiết kế chính thức: dùng phân tích thống kê chính xác và kiểm soát tốt hơn.*

---

### 9.1 Completely Randomised Design (C.R. Design)

**Core idea:** Subjects are **randomly assigned** to treatments. Randomisation is used to control for all extraneous variables — on average, they affect all groups equally.

**Principles used:** Replication + Randomisation (NOT local control)

**Assumption:** Because of random assignment, extraneous factors will affect all treatment conditions equally → any significant difference between conditions can be attributed to the IV.

**Two forms:**

#### Two-group Simple Randomised Design
- Population defined → random sample drawn → participants randomly assigned to experimental or control group
- Example: 150 students randomly split into Group A (usual training) and Group B (specialised training). Pre-test and post-test both groups. Compare gains.
- **Limitation:** Does not control individual differences among those in each group. A student in the usual training group may be more motivated and work harder, confounding results.

#### Random Replications Design
- Eliminates the limitation above by providing multiple repetitions for each treatment
- Randomises individual differences across multiple replications

---

### 9.2 Randomised Block Design (R.B. Design)

**Core idea:** When you know there's an important extraneous variable (e.g., gender, IQ, soil fertility), divide participants into **blocks** based on that variable. Then randomly assign all treatments within each block.

**Principles used:** Replication + Randomisation + Local Control

**How it works:**
1. Identify the extraneous variable you want to control (this becomes the blocking factor)
2. Divide participants into blocks — within each block, subjects are **homogeneous** on that variable
3. Number of subjects in each block = number of treatments
4. Randomly assign one subject from each block to each treatment

**Key property:** Each treatment appears the **same number of times in each block**.

**Effect:** Variability due to the blocking factor is removed from the error term → better, more precise estimate of the true treatment effect.

**Example — Vaccine Trial:**
> Problem: Men and women react differently to medication → gender is a potential extraneous variable.
>
> Solution using RBD:
> - Block 1: Men → 250 men get placebo, 250 men get vaccine
> - Block 2: Women → 250 women get placebo, 250 women get vaccine
>
> Result: Gender is no longer a source of variability — differences between treatment conditions cannot be attributed to gender.

**Example — Statistics Test:**
> 4 forms of a statistics test given to students from 5 IQ blocks (each block = 1 IQ level). One student from each IQ block randomly assigned to each test form. The order of tests randomised for each student. → IQ variation is controlled; only test form effect remains.

---

### 9.3 Latin Square Design (L.S. Design)

**Core idea:** An extension of RBD to control **TWO extraneous variables simultaneously**. Uses rows to represent one blocking factor and columns to represent the other.

**When to use:** When there are two known major extraneous sources of variability that need to be controlled at the same time.

**Structure:** A v × v grid (v rows, v columns, v treatments). Each treatment (Latin letter) appears **exactly once in each row AND exactly once in each column**.

**Example — Fertiliser experiment:**
> Problem: Want to test 4 fertilisers on crop yield. But:
> - Soil fertility varies across the field (one source of variability)
> - Seed variety also varies (another source of variability)
>
> Solution: Use a 4×4 Latin square:
> - Rows = soil fertility blocks (North → South)
> - Columns = seed variety blocks
> - Each fertiliser (A, B, C, D) appears once in each row and once in each column
>
> ```
>        Seed 1  Seed 2  Seed 3  Seed 4
> Soil 1:   A       B       C       D
> Soil 2:   B       C       D       A
> Soil 3:   C       D       A       B
> Soil 4:   D       A       B       C
> ```

**Advantage:** Both soil fertility AND seed variety effects are measured and removed from the error → only fertiliser effect remains.

**Limitations:**
- Requires rows = columns = treatments (if you have 4 fertilisers, you need a 4×4 grid)
- Assumes NO interaction between treatments and blocking factors
- 2×2 design has no degrees of freedom left for error → cannot be used
- If treatments ≥ 10: rows/columns become too large and may not be homogeneous → local control breaks down
- **Recommended range: 5×5 to 9×9**

---

### 9.4 Factorial Designs

**Core idea:** Test the effects of **multiple factors simultaneously** in one experiment — and examine both their individual effects AND their interactions.

*Kiểm tra ảnh hưởng của nhiều yếu tố cùng lúc và xem chúng tương tác với nhau như thế nào.*

#### Why use factorial designs?
- Real phenomena are affected by many factors at once
- Running separate single-factor experiments would miss interactions between factors
- More efficient: one factorial experiment replaces many single-factor experiments

#### Two types:

**Simple Factorial Design (Two-factor):**
- Examines effects of exactly **2 factors** on the dependent variable
- Also called: "two-factor-factorial design"
- Can be 2×2, 3×4, 5×3, etc. (rows × columns = levels of factor 1 × levels of factor 2)

**Complex Factorial Design (Multi-factor):**
- Examines effects of **3 or more factors** simultaneously
- Example: 2×2×2 design = 3 factors, each with 2 levels = 8 cells total
- Can determine:
  - **Main effects**: effect of each factor individually
  - **First-order interactions**: interaction between each pair of factors
  - **Second-order interactions**: interaction between factors taken in triples

#### Understanding Interaction Effects

An **interaction** occurs when the effect of one factor on the DV **depends on the level of another factor**.

**Example:**
> Factor A = Teaching method (traditional vs interactive)
> Factor B = Student level (beginner vs advanced)
> DV = Test score
>
> - If interactive method helps advanced students but makes no difference for beginners → INTERACTION EXISTS
> - If interactive method helps both beginners and advanced equally → NO INTERACTION (the lines in a graph would be parallel)

**Graph interpretation:**
- **Non-parallel lines** in a factorial plot → interaction present
- **Parallel lines** → no interaction; factors are independent of each other

#### Notation

| Notation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 2×2 | 2 factors, each with 2 levels → 4 cells |
| 3×4 | 2 factors: 3 levels × 4 levels → 12 cells |
| 2×2×2 | 3 factors, each with 2 levels → 8 cells |

**What you can measure from a 2×2 factorial:**
- Main effect of Factor A (row means — ignoring B)
- Main effect of Factor B (column means — ignoring A)
- Interaction between A and B (does the effect of A depend on B?)

#### Advantages of Factorial Designs
1. Same accuracy as single-factor experiments but with **less labour and cost**
2. Can determine main effects of **multiple factors** in ONE experiment
3. Can detect **interaction effects** — impossible to find in single-factor experiments
4. Permit various other comparisons of interest

## 10. Summary Cheat Sheet

### Complete Taxonomy of Experimental Designs

```
Experimental Designs
│
├── INFORMAL (less statistical rigour)
│     ├── Before-after WITHOUT control → 1 group, time-lapse problem
│     ├── After-only WITH control → 2 groups, no baseline problem
│     └── Before-after WITH CONTROL → 2 groups, BEST — controls both time & group differences
│
└── FORMAL (precise statistical procedures)
      ├── Completely Randomised (CR)
      │     ├── Two-group simple randomised
      │     └── Random replications design
      │     Uses: Replication + Randomisation only
      │
      ├── Randomised Block (RB) → controls 1 extraneous variable via blocks
      │     Uses: Replication + Randomisation + Local Control
      │
      ├── Latin Square (LS) → controls 2 extraneous variables simultaneously
      │     Constraint: rows = columns = treatments; best at 5×5 to 9×9
      │
      └── Factorial → tests multiple factors + interaction effects
            ├── Simple factorial (2 factors)
            └── Complex factorial (3+ factors)
```

### Three Allocation Designs

| Design | Who participates? | Main risk | Control method |
|---|---|---|---|
| Independent Measures | Different people per group | Individual differences | Random allocation |
| Repeated Measures | Same people, all conditions | Order effects | Counterbalancing |
| Matched Pairs | Matched pairs, one per group | Hard to match | Random assignment within pairs |

### Three Principles

| Principle | What it does |
|---|---|
| Replication | Repeats each treatment → more accurate, reliable estimates |
| Randomisation | Random assignment → eliminates bias, enables statistical testing |
| Local Control | Blocks known extraneous variables → removes them from error |

### Key Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Independent variable (IV) | The variable the researcher manipulates — the "cause" |
| Dependent variable (DV) | The variable the researcher measures — the "effect" |
| Extraneous variable | Any other variable that could affect DV — must be controlled |
| Condition | A specific level of the IV (e.g., high dose, low dose, no dose) |
| Experimental group | Receives the special/new treatment |
| Control group | Receives usual conditions or placebo — the baseline |
| Interaction | When effect of one factor depends on the level of another factor |
| Counterbalancing | In repeated measures: alternating condition order across participants to cancel order effects |
| Block | A group of homogeneous experimental units — used in RBD and LS designs |
| Placebo | An inert treatment given to control group — controls for psychological effects of treatment expectation |
