# Critical Literature Review

---

## 1. What is a Literature Review?

### Formal Definition
> **Creswell (2005):** "A literature review is a written summary of journal articles, books and other documents that describes the past and current state of information, organizes the literature into topics and documents a need for a proposed study." *(p. 79)*

### Practical Definition
> The **presentation, classification, and evaluation** of what other researchers have written on a particular subject.

### Key Distinction
A literature review is **NOT** a list of paper summaries.  
You must be **analytical and critical** — evaluate strengths, weaknesses, and identify gaps, rather than simply describing what you have read.

---

## 2. Why is a Literature Review Needed?

A literature review serves 3 core purposes in the research process:

1. Broaden your knowledge base
- Ensure you read widely around your subject area
- Helps you understand how your findings fit into the existing body of knowledge
- Contextualise your research within the broader field

2. Bring clarity and focus to your research problem
- Deepens your understanding of the subject area
- Helps you **conceptualise your research problem** clearly and precisely
- Reveals the **relationship between your research problem and the body of knowledge**.

3. Improve your methodology
- Reveals what procudures and methods others have used on similar problems
- Highlights what **problems others faced** with those methods
- Positions you to select a methodology capable of providing valid answers to your research questions

### Additional roles
- A **major part** of any thesis or research report submitted for assessment or publication
- Helps explain the **background context** and the importance of the research
- **Reveals gaps** in the literature and points out problems worth pursuing

## 3. How to Conduct a Literature Review

The process follows three sequential steps:

```
Step 1: Search for existing literature in your area of study
                        ↓
Step 2: Review the literature selected
                        ↓
Step 3: Develop a theoretical framework
```

---

### Step 1: Search for Existing Literature

This step involves three sub-questions:

#### A. What You Search (Where to Look)

| Source | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Books** | High quality; good for foundational knowledge; findings integrated into coherent body | Not fully up to date (years between completion and publication) |
| **Journals** | Most up-to-date; contains original work and latest developments | Often requires subscription |
| **Conference Proceedings** | Speedy publication; latest results; as important as journals in CS/IT | Quality can vary |
| **Internet (Google Scholar, DBLP)** | Convenient; free resources; various search engines | Beware of "internet junk" |

**Key databases:** IEEE Xplore, Springerlink, ScienceDirect, DBLP, Google Scholar

#### Reading Sequence by Stage

```
START   →  Encyclopaedias, Books        (build foundational knowledge)
MIDDLE  →  Review Articles              (get an overview of the field)
END     →  Journal Papers               (deep, specific understanding)
```

> **Tip:** If the area is new to you, read an **introduction to the topic** or the **introduction section of a recent journal article** before conducting a broad search.

---

#### B. What You Search It For

- Search for literature related to your **research area or project**
- Each topic can be viewed from **many different perspectives**, read broadly first, then narrow down
- Read briefly, write notes, and summarise findings (use text and comparison tables)

**Two important article types to identify:**

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Seminal articles** | First to introduce a concept to the world or to a new field |
| **Milestone articles** | Mark a significant advance or turning point in the direction of research |

**Iterative Search Process:**
1. Read abstracts briefly -> take nodes
2. Summarise in tables for comparison
3. Follow **references within papers already found** to discover more related work
4. Continuously refine your reading list
5. Produce a final summary for the literature review report

---

#### C. How You Search

- **Bibliographic databases:** IEEE Xplore, Springerlink, ScienceDirect
- **Free tools:** Google Scholar, DBLP
- **University library:** both hard copies and soft copies (e-library)
- Books, journals, conference proceedings with accurate and comprehensive descriptions

---

### Step 2: Review the Literature Selected 

#### 3-pass Reading Strategy

``` 
Pass 1: Read the Abstract ONLY
        → Does it clearly state the research focus and main findings?

Pass 2: Read Introduction + Conclustion ONLY
        → Do you now understand the paper's significance?

Pass 3: Read the whole paper (or necessary parts)
        → Deepen your understanding
        → Note: this may require several iterations
```

#### Annotated Bibliography

As you read each article, make notes covering:

- **Full bibliographic details** (in case you decide to use it)
- **Why it is relevant** to your project
- Whether it **supports or contradicts** your point of view
- Any **errors or limitations** in the article
- Your own **questions or observations** arising from the paper

> The bibliography note should be as **comprehensive as possible**.

#### Example of a Good Annotated Bibliography Entry

```
Lenstra, A. K.; Lenstra, H. W., Jr.; Lovász, L. (1982).
"Factoring polynomials with rational coefficients."
Mathematische Annalen. 261(4): 515–534.

This article introduces an efficient algorithm to compute a reduced basis
of a given integral lattice. A reduced basis has short, near-orthogonal
vectors. The authors apply this to find an efficient method for factorising
polynomials with rational coefficients.

Q: Can we generalise this algorithm for lattices over rings of integers
of number fields?
```

---

### Step 3: Develop a Theoretical Framework

- As you read, you will notice the literature deals with **multiple aspects** of your research topic
- Use these aspects as a basis for developing your **theoretical framework**
- The framework is the **foundation your research is built upon**

> *A theoretical framework is a summary of your theory regarding a particular problem, developed through a review of previous research on the variables involved.*

#### Example — Cloud Data Integrity Framework

```
Theoretical Framework: Cloud Data Integrity
├── Methods for data integrity
├── Data integrity audit
├── Data integrity models and proofs
└── ...
```

Once you develop a rough framework:
- **Slot findings** from reviewed material into the framework
- Identify which aspects are well-covered and which have **gaps**

---

## 4. Writing Up the Literature Review

### Overall Structure

| Section | Purpose | Proportion |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| **Introduction** | Overview of subject/issue/theory; objectives of the review | ~10% |
| **Body** | Critical review of literature, organised around ideas | ~80% |
| **Conclusion** | Salient points, observations, and gaps identified | ~10% |

### How to Write the Body

1. **List the main themes** that emerged while reading
2. **Convert themes into subheadings** - precise, descriptive, logically ordered
3. Under each subheading, record:
- Main findings related to the theme
- Reasons for and against arguments
- Identified **gaps and issues**

### Contextualising Your Findings
- Systematically **compare your findings** with those of others
- Quote from studies to show how your findings **contradict, confirm, or add to** them
- This section is written **after data analysis**, when discussing your own results

### Important Rule
> The literature review is organised around **ideas**, not around individual papers.
> It is **NOT** a list of summaries as in an annotated bibliography.

--- 

## 5. Forms of a Literature Review

Choose one of the following forms based on your research area:

1. Chronological
Focus on materials according to the **time they were developed**.

> **Example:** A review of face detection and recognition methods developed between 1985 and 2009.

2. Thematic
Organise round **different research aspects or themes**.

> **Example — Cloud Data Integrity:**
> - Privacy
> - Identity-based / Attribute-based approaches
> - Dynamic operations
> - ...

3. Methodological
Categorise and review the **various methods or algorithms** and how they evolved.
> **Example — Cloud Data Integrity:**
> - Privacy
> - Identity-based / Attribute-based approaches
> - Dynamic operations
> - ...

> These forms must be interpreted in the context of your specific area of research.

---

## 6. Using Tables for Comparison

After selecting the most relevant articles, use a **comparison table** to:

- Summarise their **main features** side by side
- Identify **pros and cons** of previous work
- Clearly **identify research gaps**
- Help select a suitable research project direction

### Example Table Structure

| Paper | Problem Addressed | Method Used | Key Result | Limitations |
|-------|------------------|-------------|------------|-------------|
| Author A (2020) | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Author B (2021) | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Author C (2022) | ... | ... | ... | Gap → your research |

## 7. Bibliography & BibTeX

### Best Practices
- Use a bibliographic organising tool such as **BibTeX** or **EndNote** from the start
- Maintain an **accurate record** of all papers you collected or read
- Collect papers in **and around** your field, don't be too narrow

### Types of Publications

| Type | BibTeX Entry Type |
|------|------------------|
| Book | `@book` |
| Thesis | `@phdthesis` / `@mastersthesis` |
| Report | `@techreport` |
| Journal article | `@article` |
| Conference paper | `@inproceedings` |
| Web article | `@misc` |

## 8. Analysing a Paper

When reading any paper, ask two groups of questions:

### Localization Questions (Identifying the Paper)
- What is the **publication title**?
- Who are the **authors**?
- What is the **year of publication**?
- Where does it appear — **journal, conference, book**?

### Content Questions (Understanding the Paper)

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| What **problem** is being solved and why is it important? | Understand the motivation |
| What have **others done** about this problem before? | Understand the state of the art |
| What **solution** is proposed and what **result** was obtained? | Understand the contribution |
| How does it **compare** with previous results? | Evaluate significance |
| What **further work** is proposed? | Identify open problems |
| What is the **relevance to your research**? | ← Most important question |

### Paper Structure to Navigate

```
Abstract / Synopsis / Introduction
        ↓
Body (with subsections)
        ↓
Discussion and Conclusion
```

## 9. Summary

```
CRITICAL LITERATURE REVIEW
│
├── WHAT
│   └── Presentation + Classification + Evaluation
│       of what others have written on a topic
│
├── WHY
│   ├── Broaden knowledge base
│   ├── Bring clarity and focus to research problem
│   └── Improve methodology
│
└── HOW
    ├── Step 1: SEARCH
    │   ├── What: Books → Review articles → Journal papers
    │   ├── What for: Seminal & milestone articles; broad then narrow
    │   └── How: IEEE, Springer, Google Scholar, Library
    │
    ├── Step 2: REVIEW CRITICALLY
    │   ├── 3-pass reading (Abstract → Intro+Conclusion → Full paper)
    │   └── Annotated bibliography for every paper read
    │
    ├── Step 3: DEVELOP THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
    │   └── Group aspects → Slot findings → Identify gaps
    │
    └── WRITE UP
        ├── Structure: Introduction → Body (80%) → Conclusion
        ├── Form: Chronological / Thematic / Methodological
        ├── Use comparison tables to identify gaps
        └── Organise around IDEAS, not around papers
```

### Golden Rules
1. Be **analytical and critical** — do not just describe
2. **Read in passes** — abstract first, then intro/conclusion, then full paper
3. **Take annotated notes** for every paper from the start
4. Use **BibTeX or EndNote** to manage references from day one
5. Organise around **ideas and themes**, not individual papers
6. Always ask: **"What is the relevance of this work to MY research?"**
