# Defining Research Problems

---

## 1. The Research Process

The research process consists of a series of steps or actions required for effectively conducting research. These steps provide useful procedural guidelines for conducting research in a systematic and rigorous manner.

### Research Process Flow

```
I.   Define research problem
          ├── Review concepts and theories        ← (Step II feeds back here)
          └── Review previous research findings
II.  Review the literature
III. Formulate hypotheses
IV.  Design research (including sample design)   ←── FF (feed forward)
V.   Collect data (Execution)                    ←── F  (feedback)
VI.  Analyse data (Test hypotheses if any)       ←── F  (feedback)
VII. Interpret and report                        ←── F  (feedback)

F  = Feedback    — helps control the sub-system to which it is transmitted
FF = Feed Forward — serves the vital function of providing criteria for evaluation
```

### What Makes People Undertake Research?

- Desire to get a research degree along with its consequential benefits
- Desire to face the challenge in solving unsolved problems — concern over practical problems initiates research
- Desire to get intellectual joy of doing some creative work
- Desire to be of service to society
- Desire to get respectability

## 2. Defining the Research Problem

Defining the research problem is the **first and most crucial step** in the research process. Its main function is to decide what you want to find out about. The way you formulate a problem determines almost every step that follows.

### Why Is Definition Important?
- The definition of a problem **sets the direction** of the study
- The definition **reveals the methodology** or procedure of the study

### What Is a Research Problem?

> A research problem is one which requires a researcher to find out the best solution for the given problem — that is, to find out by which course of action the objective can be attained **optimally** in the context of a given environment.

---

## 3. Components of a Research Problem

According to Kothari (2004), a valid research problem must satisfy **five essential conditions**:

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Individual or Group** | There must be an individual or a group which has some difficulty or problem |
| **Objective(s)** | There must be some objective(s) to be attained. If one wants nothing, one cannot have a problem |
| **Alternative Means** | There must be at least **two** alternative means (courses of action) for obtaining the objective(s). With no choice of means, there is no problem |
| **Some Doubt** | There must remain some doubt in the mind of the researcher regarding the selection of alternatives. The researcher must evaluate the relative efficiency of possible alternatives |
| **Environment** | There must be some environment(s) to which the difficulty pertains |

> Reference: Kothari, C.R 2004, *Research Methodology: Methods and Techniques*, 2nd edn, New Age International Publisher, India.

---

## 4. Identifying the Research Problem

After completing the literature review, a researcher should have a thorough understanding of the **state-of-the-art** of the research area. Research problems are typically identified from:

- **Open problems** — problems not yet solved according to the literature survey
- **Drawbacks in previous work** — limitations, weaknesses, or inefficiencies in existing approaches
- **Need of a new method** — due to new requirements, new environments, or new technologies

---

## 5. Criteria for Defining the Research Problem

A research problem must satisfy the following **seven criteria** before proceeding:

| # | Criterion | Key Considerations |
|---|-----------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Novelty** | Solving an open problem; significant improvement over previous work; or non-trivial implementation of known theory |
| 2 | **Importance for the Field** | Significance for the research field; practical value in terms of application and implementation; IT/CS research has an obligation to deliver social and economic impact |
| 3 | **Availability of Data and Method** | Data must meet standards of accuracy and verifiability; a suitable method or tool must be available or developed |
| 4 | **Potentially Solvable** | Set aims realistically based on your knowledge and literature review; consult your supervisor; consider data, method, duration, and financial constraints |
| 5 | **Advantage Over Existing Methods** | Results must lead to significantly new findings or significant improvement over the current state of the art |
| 6 | **Financial Support** | For large projects, funding must be available for equipment, research assistants, and other resources |
| 7 | **Time Factor** | A realistic time frame must exist; correctly estimate the project size before committing |

### On Novelty — What Counts as Novel?

The question of novelty is **not merely** one of avoiding duplication of earlier investigations. A contribution is novel if it satisfies at least one of the following:

1. **Solving an open problem** — a problem that has not been solved according to the literature survey
2. **Significant improvement over previous work** — a clearly better result in efficiency, security, accuracy, etc.
3. **Non-trivial implementation of known theory** — e.g.:
   - Designing a suitable experiment
   - Solving an implementation problem
   - Matching theory to a real-world setting

## 6. How to State a Research Problem

A well-stated research problem must include the following elements:

- **Description of the general nature of the problem** — why it is a problem, why it is significant, and how it contributes to the body of knowledge
- **Statement of limitations** of the technique(s) currently employed
- **Importance-value or significance** of the study
- Must be stated **"clearly and unambiguously in question form"**

### Example: Levels of Problem Statement Quality

The following example (Kothari, 2004) illustrates the difference between a vague and a precise research problem:

| Quality | Problem Statement | Issues |
|---------|------------------|--------|
| ❌ Too Vague | "Why is productivity in Japan so much higher than in India?" | Ambiguous — which type of productivity? Which industries? Which time period? |
| ⚠️ Better | "What factors were responsible for the higher labour productivity of Japan's manufacturing industries during 1971–1980 relative to India's manufacturing industries?" | More specific, but still lacks the quantitative dimension |
| ✅ Best | "To what extent did labour productivity in 1971–1980 in Japan exceed that of India in respect of 15 selected manufacturing industries? What factors were responsible for the productivity differentials between the two countries by industries?" | Clear, unambiguous, quantifiable, and in question form |

**Key takeaway:** The more specific and quantifiable the problem statement, the clearer the direction of the research.

## 7. Exercise — Face Recognition System

The lecture used a **face recognition system** as a brainstorming exercise to demonstrate how a broad topic is broken into specific, researchable questions. This illustrates how a general topic gives rise to many distinct research problems across different sub-stages.

### Face Recognition Process

```
INPUT IMAGE / VIDEO SEQUENCE
          ↓
   FACE DETECTION  ──→  TRACKING  ──→  RECOGNITION  ──→  IDENTITY
```

### Brainstorming Research Questions by Stage

| Stage | Sample Research Questions |
|-------|--------------------------|
| **Face Detection** | What features are extracted from the input to detect faces? What problems make it difficult to extract them? What methods/algorithms are used? |
| **Tracking** | How is the detected face tracked? What features are tracked and how? What methods/algorithms are used? Once tracked, what features are used for recognition? |
| **Recognition** | How are features extracted for recognition? What difficulties are encountered? Are there workarounds? What methods/algorithms are used? |
| **Evaluation** | How are detection, tracking, and recognition algorithms evaluated? What databases are used for evaluation? |

> **Note:** These are questions arising from a brainstorming exercise. The next step is to conduct a **literature review** to determine which questions remain open or unsolved — those become the research problem.

### Golden Rules

1. The research problem is the **most important step** — it determines every step that follows
2. A problem must be **novel** — solve an open problem, improve on prior work, or implement theory non-trivially
3. State the problem **clearly and unambiguously in question form**
4. **Brainstorm** many questions from your topic, then use the literature review to identify which are truly open
5. Always check **all 7 criteria** before committing to a research problem
