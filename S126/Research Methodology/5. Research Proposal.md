## PART 1: RESEARCH PROPOSAL

---

## 1. What is a Research Proposal?

A research proposal is a formal document that **explains the nature and extent of your planned research**. It is written for an academic reader (e.g., supervisor or peer researcher) and serves two key purposes:

- **For the reader** — communicates what you plan to research and why it matters
- **For the researcher** — acts as a guideline and helps clarify ideas, spot potential issues, and assess feasibility

> A research proposal presents your **idea and expected outcomes** — it does NOT answer your research question. That is what the actual research will do.

A good proposal clearly addresses:
- **The WHAT** — your research idea, question, and expected outcomes
- **The WHY** — why the question is significant and what value it brings to the field

---

## 2. Why is a Research Proposal Important?

A research proposal formally outlines your intended research, covering:

- Your **approach and methodology**
- **Timeline and feasibility**
- All **resources and considerations** needed to progress the research

Think of it as a tool to **clarify your idea** and make conducting the actual research easier and more structured.

---

## 3. Structure of a Research Proposal

A complete research proposal includes these essential parts:

| # | Section | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Background of Research** | Context and motivation for the study |
| 2 | **Statement of the Problem** | The gap or issue being addressed |
| 3 | **Critical Review of Literature** | Summary and analysis of existing work |
| 4 | **Research Problems & Hypotheses** | Core questions and expected outcomes |
| 5 | **Aims and Objectives** | What the research intends to achieve |
| 6 | **Methodology and Procedure** | How the research will be conducted |
| 7 | **Implications / Significance** | Why the research matters |
| 8 | **Work Schedule** | Timeline and milestones |
| 9 | **References** | Bibliography of all cited sources |
| 10 | **Budget and Resources** | Estimated costs and required resources |

---

## 4. Introduction & Literature Review

The proposal begins with an introduction that includes a **literature review** — one of the most critical sections. It serves two functions:

1. **Broadens knowledge base** — by reviewing available literature in your study area
2. **Informs methodology** — by showing what methods others have used and what has worked or failed

### The Critical Review of Related Literature

- Summarises previous research to identify **what is already known** and what is **still unknown**
- Helps **avoid duplication** of existing work
- Provides a basis for **formulating the research problem and hypotheses**
- Helps determine whether the proposed research is **significant and original**

---

## 5. Statement of the Problem

This section identifies the **gap** in existing knowledge that your research will fill. It should:

- Identify **unanswered questions** in the literature
- Identify the **issues** that are the basis of your study
- Specify any **differences of opinion** in the literature regarding these questions
- Develop a **rationale** explaining how your study will fill the identified gaps

---

## 6. Hypotheses

A hypothesis is a **testable statement** about an assumed relationship between two variables or about a phenomenon.

Key points:
- A scientific study is built around hypotheses
- Hypotheses indicate the **expected outcomes** of the investigation
- Should first be stated in **positive form**
- Help define the **direction of the research** and shape the objectives

---

## 7. Aims and Objectives

Objectives are broken into two levels:

| Level | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Main Objective** | Indicates the central problem of the study |
| **Sub-objectives** | Identify the specific issues to be examined |

**Guidelines for writing objectives:**
- Each sub-objective should address **only one issue**
- Use **action-oriented verbs**: *to determine, to find out, to ascertain, to compare, to assess*
- Sub-objectives should be **numerically listed**
- In **qualitative studies**, objectives are less rigid — state an overall aim and allow flexibility to explore

**Example (Qualitative):**
> *"What are the special challenges that students born in Germany with an immigrant background face?"*
> → This is a valid qualitative question, but it should be **narrowed** to one specific immigrant group for feasibility.

---

## 8. Methodology and Procedure

This section is the **full research plan** and answers:

- **How** will you answer your research questions?
  - e.g., case study, descriptive, experimental, or non-experimental design
- **How** will you carry out the research to fill the identified gap?
- **What tools and methods** will be used?
- **How** will you validate your findings and methods?
- **How** will you demonstrate the applicability of your findings?

It must describe:
- Sample and population
- Data-gathering devices and tools
- How sources of data will be selected
- How data will be analysed and conclusions drawn

---

## 9. Ethical Issues

### What is Research Ethics?

Ethics are the **rules and values** (written and unwritten) that govern expected behaviour — in research, these ensure integrity, fairness, and protection of participants.

**Research ethics** govern how scientific research is **performed and disseminated** at academic institutions.

### Why Research Ethics Matter

| Reason | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Promotes research aims** | Supports knowledge expansion |
| **Supports collaboration** | Ensures mutual respect and fairness between researchers |
| **Accountability** | Researchers (often publicly funded) must justify their actions |
| **Public trust** | People must be confident in research to support and fund it |
| **Social and moral values** | Upholds the principle of doing no harm to others |

**In your proposal:**
- Identify any **ethical issues** related to your study
- Describe how you propose to **deal with potential harm** to respondents
- All institutions have an **ethics policy** — make sure your research complies

---

## 10. Work Schedule

A work schedule sets out a **timeline** for completing all stages of the research.

- List each operational step and assign a **completion date**
- Develop a **chart or timetable** (commonly a **Gantt chart**)

**Gantt Chart** — a visual bar chart that maps tasks against time, showing:
- What tasks need to be done
- When each task starts and ends
- How tasks overlap or depend on each other

---

## 11. References

The last section provides a **full bibliography** of all sources cited, including:
- Books
- Academic journals
- Conference proceedings
- Online sources

---

## 12. Budget and Resources

If applying for funding, the budget should cover:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **A. Salaries & Wages** | Researchers, assistants, consultants, analysts, technicians |
| **B. Equipment** | Fixed, movable, office equipment, installation |
| **C. Materials & Supplies** | Office supplies, questionnaire forms, lab supplies, data access |
| **D. Travel** | Conferences, fieldwork, sponsor meetings, mileage |
| **E. Services** | Computer use, duplication, publication costs, data analysis |
| **F. Other** | Space rental, subcontracts, tuition, participant reimbursement |

> Note: Students may not always need to include a full budget section.

---

## 13. What Makes a Good Proposal?

- Research is **unique** or offers **new insight**
- Title, aims, and objectives are **clear and well-defined**
- **Comprehensive literature review** has been conducted
- **Good match** between the research problem and the approach adopted
- Researcher demonstrates **relevant background knowledge**
- **Timetable, resources, and budget** are thoroughly planned
- Has **practical implications** for the field

---

## 14. Tips for Writing a Research Proposal

- Use **confident language**: *"It is clear that…"* rather than *"It seems that…"*
- **Break up large text blocks** with sub-headings and bullet points
- **Anticipate limitations** and address them directly
- Make the proposal **easy to skim** — use sub-headings and restate key ideas
- Study examples of proposals in your field for:
  - How titles are devised
  - How the proposal is structured
  - Discipline-specific language used

## 1. What is LaTeX?

- **TeX** is a typesetting program created by **Donald Knuth** (started 1977, released 1982)
- **LaTeX** (by Leslie Lamport) is a macro package built on top of TeX that enables authors to produce **highest-quality typographical output** using predefined professional layouts
- LaTeX uses the TeX formatter as its **typesetting engine**
- Available on all platforms: **Unix/Linux, Windows, MacOS**

---

## 2. Installing LaTeX

| Platform | Popular Installation |
|---|---|
| Windows | **MiKTeX** + WinEdt or TeXinCenter |
| Linux/Unix | Most distributions include LaTeX |
| MacOS | **teTeX** |

**Required additional tools (Windows):**
- Ghostscript and GSView
- Adobe Acrobat Reader
- MiKTeX
- WinEdt (shareware) or TeXinCenter (free)

---

## 3. The LaTeX Workflow

```
Create .tex file
      ↓
latex document.tex       → produces document.dvi
      ↓
dvi2ps document.dvi      → produces document.ps
      ↓
ps2pdf document.ps       → produces document.pdf

OR use directly:
pdflatex document.tex    → produces document.pdf
```

---

## 4. Basic Document Structure

Every LaTeX document follows this structure:

```latex
\documentclass[options]{type}
\usepackage{...}           % Load packages

\begin{document}
  % Your content goes here
\end{document}
```

### Document Classes

| Class | Use Case |
|---|---|
| `article` | Journals, presentations, short reports |
| `report` | Long reports, theses, small books |
| `book` | Full books |
| `slides` | Presentation slides |

### Common Options

```latex
\documentclass[a4paper, 11pt]{article}
```

| Option | Description |
|---|---|
| `10pt, 11pt, 12pt` | Font size |
| `a4paper, letterpaper` | Paper size |
| `twocolumn` | Two-column layout |
| `twoside, oneside` | Double or single-sided printing |

---

## 5. Document Hierarchy

### Article class structure:
```
\section{}
  \subsection{}
    \subsubsection{}
```

### Report class structure:
```
\chapter{}
  \section{}
    \subsection{}
```

Both `article` and `report` support: `\title{}`, `\author{}`, `\date{}`

Report additionally supports: `\tableofcontents`, `\listoffigures`, `\index`

---

## 6. Example: Article Document

```latex
\documentclass[a4paper, 11pt]{article}
\usepackage{latexsym}
\author{A. Good Student}
\title{My Article}

\begin{document}
\maketitle
\tableofcontents

\section{Introduction}
The use of eigenvalues in statistical image processing is so pervasive.

\section{Conclusion}
We need to understand both theoretical and intuitive interpretations.
\end{document}
```

## 7. Including Graphics

Two packages are available:

```latex
\usepackage{graphics}   % Standard
\usepackage{graphicx}   % Extended (recommended)
```

**Basic usage:**
```latex
\includegraphics[options]{filename}
```

**Common options:**

| Option | Description |
|---|---|
| `width=3in` | Set width |
| `height=2in` | Set height |
| `scale=0.5` | Scale by a factor |
| `angle=30` | Rotate (positive = counterclockwise) |

**Placing in a figure environment:**
```latex
\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=2in]{graph.png}
  \caption{Output of Eigen-analysis}
  \label{fig:graph}
\end{figure}
```

> Note: Use `pdflatex` to include `.jpg`, `.png`, and `.pdf` images.

## 9. Writing Math Equations

LaTeX is powerful for typesetting mathematical formulas.

| Command | Output |
|---|---|
| `$...$` | Inline math |
| `\[...\]` | Display math, no number |
| `\begin{equation}...\end{equation}` | Numbered equation |
| `\begin{eqnarray}...\end{eqnarray}` | Multiple aligned equations |

**Examples:**

```latex
% Inline
$\lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{6}$

% Numbered equation
\begin{equation}
  \forall x \in \mathbf{R}: \quad x^{2} \geq 0
\end{equation}

% Multiple aligned equations
\begin{eqnarray}
  f(x)  &=& \cos x \\
  f'(x) &=& -\sin x \\
  \int_{0}^{x} f(y)dy &=& \sin x
\end{eqnarray}
```

## 10. Bibliography with BibTeX

LaTeX handles bibliography through the **BibTeX** module, allowing consistent citation styles.

### BibTeX Entry Types

**Journal article:**
```bibtex
@ARTICLE{SMITH99,
  AUTHOR  = {A. Smith and B. Conrad},
  TITLE   = {Perceptual Organization and Image Segmentation},
  JOURNAL = {IEEE Trans. Image Processing},
  YEAR    = {1989},
  volume  = {3},
  pages   = {189-197},
}
```

**Book:**
```bibtex
@BOOK{JAIN98,
  AUTHOR    = {A. K. Jain},
  TITLE     = {Fundamentals of Image Processing},
  PUBLISHER = {Prentice-Hall},
  YEAR      = {1998},
  address   = {New York},
}
```

**Conference proceedings:**
```bibtex
@INPROCEEDINGS{LI05,
  AUTHOR    = {W. Li and M. Shi and P. Ogunbona},
  TITLE     = {A new divide and conquer algorithm...},
  BOOKTITLE = {IEEE Multimedia Signal Processing Workshop},
  YEAR      = {2005},
  pages     = {585-588},
}
```

### Citing in Document

```latex
"...graphs allow a hierarchical representation \cite{LI05}"
```

### Adding Bibliography to Document

```latex
\bibliographystyle{ieeetrans}
\bibliography{mybib}
```
