# Lecture 7 – Data Collection

---

## 1. Definition

- **Data**: Various kinds of information formatted in a particular way
- **Data collection**: The process of gathering, measuring, and analyzing accurate data from a variety of relevant sources to find answers to research problems, answer questions, evaluate outcomes, and forecast trends and probabilities

---

## 2. Why Data Collection Matters

- Society is highly dependent on data
- Accurate data collection is necessary to make informed business decisions, ensure quality assurance, and keep research integrity
- Researchers must identify: the **data types**, the **sources of data**, and the **methods** being used
- Heavy reliance on data collection in research, commercial, and government fields

---

## 3. Significance of Data Collection (5 Reasons)

| Reason | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Research Integrity** | Ensures the integrity of the research question is maintained |
| **Reduce errors** | Correct use of appropriate methods reduces likelihood of errors in results |
| **Decision Making** | Accurate data minimises risk of uninformed decisions |
| **Save Cost and Time** | Saves time and funds that would otherwise be misspent |
| **Support new ideas** | Provides evidence to support new ideas, changes, and innovations |

---

## 4. Before Collecting Data — 4 Questions to Ask

1. What is the **goal or purpose** of this research?
2. What **kinds of data** are you planning to gather?
3. What is the **collection timeframe**?
4. What **methods and procedures** will be used to collect, store, and process the information?

## 6. Primary vs Secondary vs Third-party Data

### Primary Data
- Original, **first-hand data** collected directly by the researcher
- Initial information-gathering step before any further research
- Results are **highly accurate**
- Downside: **time-consuming and expensive**

### Secondary Data
- **Second-hand data** collected by other parties; already statistically analysed
- Information the researcher tasks others to collect, or looks up themselves
- More **efficient and less expensive** than primary
- Downside: raises concerns about **accuracy and authenticity**
- Quantitative data makes up the majority of secondary data

### Third-party Data
- Aggregated data **rented or sold** by organisations with no connection to your company/users
- Nonprofit and government entities often specialise in this
- Research from public sources is usually **free**

### Key Comparison

| | Primary | Secondary | Third-party |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Source** | You collect it | Others collected it | Aggregated by unrelated orgs |
| **Accuracy** | Highest | Lower | Varies |
| **Cost** | Expensive | Cheaper | Often free |
| **Time** | Time-consuming | Efficient | Efficient |
| **Value** | Most valuable | Less valuable | Least connected to your research |

> Rule of thumb: First determine whether your research questions have already been asked and answered. If yes, you may not need primary data. Always consider time, costs, and the research question.

| | Qualitative | Quantitative |

## 9. Observations

**Definition**: Collecting information without asking questions. The researcher goes to the field and observes personally.

- Observation is characterised as the **fundamental of all research methods**
- Includes both human activities and the physical settings
- Should be done systematically, going beyond personal impressions
- More subjective — requires the researcher to add their own judgment

### Advantages of Observation
- Subjective bias eliminated if done accurately
- Captures **current, real-time** behaviour (not complicated by past or future)
- Can determine the **dynamics** of a situation
- **Independent** of respondents' willingness to respond
- Can be combined with other data sources

### Disadvantages of Observation
- **Expensive and time-consuming**
- Information provided is **very limited**
- Unforeseen factors may interfere
- Cannot record respondents' opinions on certain subjects

---

### 9a. Structured vs Unstructured Observation

| | Structured (Systematic) | Unstructured |
|---|---|---|
| **Approach** | Characterised recording style, standardised conditions, defined units | No pre-thought framework |
| **When to use** | Descriptive studies | Exploratory studies |

---

### 9b. Controlled vs Uncontrolled Observation

| | Controlled | Uncontrolled |
|---|---|---|
| **Setting** | Laboratory or pre-arranged conditions | Natural environment |
| **Instruments** | Precision instruments used | No precision instruments |
| **Aim** | Accuracy and standardisation | Spontaneous picture of life |

#### Controlled Observation (deep dive)
- Usually structured; likely carried out in a laboratory
- Researcher decides: **where, when, who, what circumstances**
- Systematically classifies behaviour into distinct categories using **coding** → can be turned into statistics
- Usually **overt** (participants know they are being observed)
- Usually **non-participant** (researcher keeps distance)

**Strengths:**
- Can be easily replicated
- Data is easier and quicker to analyse (quantitative output)
- Quick to conduct → large sample → generalisable findings

**Limitations:**
- Lacks validity — participants may **act differently** when they know they are being watched (Hawthorne effect)

#### Naturalistic Observation (Unstructured)
- Studies spontaneous behaviour in **natural surroundings**
- Researcher simply records what they see

**Strengths:**
- Greater **ecological validity** (behaviour in its own setting)
- Generates new ideas; reveals avenues not previously considered

**Limitations:**
- Small scale → may lack representative sample → findings hard to generalise
- Less reliable — variables cannot be controlled → hard to replicate
- Researcher needs training to recognise psychologically significant aspects

---

### 9c. Participant vs Non-participant Observation

| | Participant | Non-participant |
|---|---|---|
| **Role** | Observer is a member of the group | Observer does not participate |
| **Subtypes** | Covert (identity concealed) / Overt (identity revealed) | — |

**Limitations of participant observation:**
- Hard to take notes without blowing cover (covert) → relies on memory → may forget details
- Risk of losing objectivity if too involved — may "see" what they expect or want to see (selective reporting → reduced validity)

## 10. Interviews

**Definition**: Presentation of oral-verbal questions and oral-verbal responses. One of the most common and powerful ways to understand human beings.

**Forms:**
- Individual
- Face-to-face group
- Mailed / self-administered questionnaires
- Telephone surveys

**Types:** Structured | Semi-structured | Unstructured

---

### 10a. Unstructured Interview

- Also called "discovery interview" or "informal interview"
- No predetermined questions or standardised recording techniques
- Interviewer has freedom to ask supplementary questions, omit questions, change sequence
- Very flexible

**Strengths:**
- Flexible — questions adapt to respondents' answers
- Generates rich qualitative data through open questions
- Increases validity — can probe for deeper understanding and allow interviewee to steer

**Limitations:**
- Time-consuming to conduct and analyse
- Requires trained interviewers (establishing rapport, knowing when to probe) — expensive

---

### 10b. Structured Interview

- Predetermined questions + highly standardised recording techniques
- Follows a rigid procedure, fixed order
- Questions are **close-ended**

**Strengths:**
- Easy to replicate → easy to test for reliability
- Quick to conduct → large sample → generalisable

**Limitations:**
- Not flexible — no new questions can be asked
- Lacks detail — closed questions only generate quantitative data; cannot explain why a person behaves a certain way

---

### 10c. Semi-structured Interview

- Commonly used in **qualitative research** (including grounded theory)
- Same questions asked in each interview → allows comparison
- But remains flexible → other important information can still emerge
- Uses an **interview schedule** (list of specific questions or topics)
- Not all questions designed ahead — majority created during the interview
- Schedule is updated and revised after each interview in grounded theory research

---

## 11. Focus Groups

**Definition**: A group interview where all participants have something in common. Also called discussion groups.

- Provide the same type of data as in-person interviews, but add a **social element**
- Useful for examining **complex issues**
- Led by a **moderator/facilitator** who:
  - Introduces the topic
  - Asks specific questions
  - Controls digressions
  - Stops break-away conversations
  - Ensures no one person dominates

**Strengths:**
- Generates rich qualitative data through open questions
- Participants may feel more comfortable in a group → increased validity

**Limitations:**
- Hard to maintain confidentiality in a group setting
- Less reliable — open questions + potential deviation from schedule → hard to replicate
- Risk of **social desirability bias** — participants may lie to impress others
- Risk of **group think** — one or two participants may dominate
- Time-consuming and requires a skilled facilitator

---

## 12. Questionnaires & Surveys

**Three basic types:**
1. **Closed-ended** — pre-decided response categories
2. **Open-ended** — blank space for free-text response
3. **Combination** — mix of both

---

### 12a. Closed-ended Questionnaires

**Strengths:**
- Economical — large amounts of data for low cost → large, representative sample
- Data easily converted to quantitative → statistical analysis possible
- Standardised → easy to replicate → check reliability

**Limitations:**
- Lack detail — fixed responses don't capture true feelings

---

### 12b. Open-ended Questionnaires

**Strengths:**
- Rich qualitative data — can find out why a person holds a certain attitude

**Limitations:**
- Time-consuming to collect (longer to complete)
- Time-consuming to analyse (coding required)
- Not suitable for less educated respondents

---

### 12c. Combination

- Begin with closed questions (tick boxes, ranking scales), end with open questions
- Captures both "how many" and "what do they think"

---

## 13. Steps to Design a Questionnaire (6 Steps)

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **Step 1** | Define the aims — write primary and secondary aims, one sentence each. Define the target population. |
| **Step 2** | Define variables to be collected — attitudes, needs, behaviour, demographics. Translate into measurable variables. |
| **Step 3** | Review literature — identify existing validated questionnaires related to your study's aims (saves time, enables comparison). |
| **Step 4** | Compose a draft — determine mode of administration; write more questions than you'll need; place most important items in the first half. |
| **Step 5** | Revise — shorten the set; refine wording; test with variety of respondents; ensure natural flow; verify familiar terms. |
| **Step 6** | Assemble the final questionnaire — state purpose, data use, instructions, confidentiality policy; group related questions; include white space. |

---

## 14. Questionnaire Design Checklist (Do's and Don'ts)

### Do's
- Make it as **short** as possible
- Start with **easy questions**, keep complex ones for the end
- Ask for **personal information at the end**
- Use a **mix of question formats**
- Use **specific time frames** ("in the past month") and **specific place frames**
- Provide all **possible responses** in closed questions
- Use **larger font** (e.g., 14pt), high contrast (black on white)
- Include **white space** for clarity and better response rate

### Don'ts
- Don't ask **double-barrelled questions** (two questions in one)
- Don't use **negative questions** ("not" in them → confusing)
- Don't use **technical words**
- Don't use **words with multiple meanings**
- Don't ask **leading questions** (assumes a certain answer)
- Don't use **vague words** like "often" or "sometimes"
- Don't **cause offence, frustration, sadness, or anger**
- Don't use **words with emotional connotations**
- **Avoid branching** as much as possible (if/then structures → confusing)

---

## 15. Enhancing Response Rate

- Edit with **saliency** in mind — apparent relevance, importance, and interest to the respondent
- Consider **pre-notifying** sampled individuals or sending **reminders**
- If possible, **offer an incentive**

### Non-responders
- Understand characteristics of those who did not respond — to quantify bias
- Moser and Kalton suggest: those who take **longest to return** the survey are most like non-responders

---

## 16. Displaying Qualitative Data

### Narrating Participants' Words
1. **Short passages** — interspersed within paragraphs alongside the author's own understanding
2. **Longer presentations** — covering multiple paragraphs; used for special cases, meaningful scenes, or in-depth material

### Tables and Lists
- Tables: represent two dimensions (rows and columns)
- Lists: a one-column version of a table

### Graphics
- **Maps, photographs**
- **Charts** — curves, histograms, pie charts, scatter plots (for numerical data)
- **Diagrams** — process flow, hierarchical diagrams (for concepts and terms)
- Goal: represent data or concepts **clearly and precisely**
