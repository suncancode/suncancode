## 1. What is a Case Study?

### Three Complementary Definitions

**Definition 1 — Scope:**
> A case study selects a **small geographical area** or a **very limited number of individuals** as subjects of study.

*Phạm vi nhỏ — một khu vực địa lý hoặc một số lượng rất hạn chế cá nhân.*

**Definition 2 — Process:**
> A process or record of research into the development of a **particular person, group, or situation over a period of time**.
> - Example: *"A case study was undertaken over a period of two months through a series of visits to the database lab."*

*Nghiên cứu theo dõi sự phát triển của một người, nhóm, hoặc tình huống theo thời gian.*

**Definition 3 — Purpose:**
> A particular instance of something used or analysed in order to **illustrate a principle**.
> - Example: *"A case study of the effects of the new computing facility."*

*Một ví dụ cụ thể được phân tích để minh họa một nguyên tắc.*

### The Key Idea
A case study is **depth over breadth** — instead of surveying thousands of people shallowly, you examine one or a few cases in exhaustive detail within their real-world context.

*Nghiên cứu trường hợp là chiều sâu thay vì chiều rộng — thay vì khảo sát hàng nghìn người nông cạn, bạn xem xét một hoặc vài trường hợp một cách chi tiết trong bối cảnh thực tế của chúng.*

## 2. Why Use Case Study Method?

Six reasons to choose case study:

| Reason | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Test theories in the real world** | Useful for testing whether scientific theories and models actually work outside the laboratory |
| **Contextual analysis** | Explores real-life phenomena through detailed contextual analysis of a limited number of events or conditions |
| **Deep data examination** | Enables the researcher to closely examine data within a specific context |
| **Beyond numbers** | Allows going beyond quantitative statistical results to understand behavioural conditions through the actor's own perspective |
| **Mixed data** | By including both quantitative AND qualitative data, explains both the process AND the outcome |
| **Contemporary phenomena** | Investigates current, real-world situations — not abstract or historical ones |

> **Professor's insight:** The key advantage of case study is its ability to answer "how" and "why" questions about real-world events — something surveys and statistical methods cannot do.
>
> *Lợi thế chính của nghiên cứu trường hợp là khả năng trả lời câu hỏi "như thế nào" và "tại sao" về các sự kiện thực tế.*

## 3. Six Types of Case Study

### Overview Table

| Type | Core Question | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Community study | Who are these people and how do they live? | Study of Eskimo communities |
| Causal comparative | What factors caused this? | Mobile security awareness and user education |
| Activity analysis | What activities does a person perform? | How iPhone 15 users interact with iCloud |
| Content/document analysis | What do documents reveal? | Analysis of company annual reports |
| Follow-up study | What happened after the intervention? | Patients after a new depression therapy |
| Trend study | What was, is, and will be happening? | Social media usage trends over 10 years |

---

### 3.1 Community Studies

**Definition:** Analysis of a **group of people living together** in a particular geographic location in a cooperative way.

*Phân tích một nhóm người sống cùng nhau tại một địa điểm địa lý cụ thể.*

**What it covers:**
- Location and appearance
- Prevailing economic activity
- Climate and natural resources
- Historical development
- How the people live
- Social structure, goals, and life values
- Evaluation of social institutions that meet human needs

**The community itself serves as the case under investigation.**

**Classic example:** The Eskimo community study — examining how Inuit communities adapt to extreme Arctic conditions, their social structures, economic activities (hunting, fishing), and cultural values.

---

### 3.2 Causal Comparative Studies

**Definition:** Finding answers to problems through the **analysis of causal relationships**.

*Tìm câu trả lời cho vấn đề thông qua phân tích mối quan hệ nhân quả.*

**Two key questions:**
1. What **factors** seem to be associated with certain occurrences, conditions, or behaviours?
2. What is the **relative importance** of these factors?

**Example:** Study of mobile security awareness.
- Investigate: what factors are common among users who have poor mobile security habits?
- Is it educational background? Age? Technical experience?
- The case study finds patterns and relationships — not through controlled experiments, but through detailed comparative analysis.

---

### 3.3 Activity Analysis

**Definition:** Analysis of the **activities or processes** that an individual is called upon to perform, in industry or social contexts.

*Phân tích các hoạt động hoặc quy trình mà một cá nhân thực hiện.*

**Examples:**
- Analysis of individual users who use iPhone 15 → specifically, how they use iCloud
- Analysis of individual workers on a PC assembly line → examining how they use a new tool

**Use case:** Identifying inefficiencies, training needs, or usability issues in real operational contexts.

---

### 3.4 Content or Document Analysis

**Definition:** Systematic examination of **current records or documents** as sources of data.

*Kiểm tra có hệ thống các hồ sơ hoặc tài liệu hiện tại như là nguồn dữ liệu.*

**Purpose:** Should serve a useful purpose — adding important knowledge to a field or yielding helpful information.

**Three types of sources:**

| Source Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Public Records** | Transcripts, statements of purpose, yearly reports, strategy manuals, ebooks |
| **Personal Documents** | Messages, contracts, articles, social media posts, daily papers |
| **Physical Evidence** | Flyers, publications, books, printed training materials |

---

### 3.5 Follow-up Study

**Definition:** Investigates individuals **after** they have completed a programme, treatment, or course — to know its long-term impact.

*Điều tra cá nhân sau khi hoàn thành chương trình, điều trị hoặc khóa học — để biết tác động lâu dài của nó.*

**Purpose:** By examining their status or seeking their opinions, gain some idea of the adequacy or inadequacy of the research/treatments.

**Three essential components:**
1. **Exposure** — what were they exposed to? (the treatment/programme)
2. **Time** — how long after the exposure? (the follow-up interval)
3. **Outcome** — what happened to them? (the measured result)

**Example:** A follow-up study conducted on patients who had received a new type of therapy aimed at treating depression. Researchers contact patients 6 months and 12 months after therapy to measure depression levels, quality of life, and relapse rates.

---

### 3.6 Trend Studies

**Definition:** A predictive/descriptive study examining past patterns to describe the present and **predict the future**.

*Nghiên cứu dự đoán xem xét các mẫu trong quá khứ để mô tả hiện tại và dự đoán tương lai.*

**Three temporal dimensions:**
1. What has been happening **in the past**?
2. What does the **present** situation reveal on the basis of these data?
3. What is **likely to happen in the future**?

**Example:** Trend study on social media usage — examining user growth over 10 years, current platform preferences, and predicting future platform shifts.

## 4. Five Steps of Case Study Research

### The Process

```
Step 1: Choose the research problem
            ↓
Step 2: Collect data
            ↓
Step 3: Identify causal factors
            ↓
Step 4: Analyse
            ↓
Step 5: Write up
```

---

### Step 1: Choose the Research Problem

Four criteria for problem selection:
- **Identify a problem of interest** — genuinely interesting and researchable
- **Suitability for case study** — does this problem require contextual, in-depth analysis?
- **Suitability of materials** — are there documents, people, and data available?
- **Suitable study environment** — can you access the location and participants?

---

### Step 2: Collect Data

**Main method:** Interviews — the backbone of most case studies.

**Interview guidelines:**
- Prepare questions that help understand participants' opinions
- Use **open-ended questions** (cannot be answered just with 'yes' or 'no')
- Sort through collected information: what is relevant? what isn't?
- Continuously reassess: does the problem need modification? Do you need more data?

---

### Step 3: Identify Causal Factors

- **Break the problem down into its parts**
- Analyse the information in each part
- Think about what the answers to the problem might be
- Look for patterns, connections, and relationships between parts

---

### Step 4: Analyse the Results

Key characteristics of case study analysis:

- More **opinion-based** than statistical analysis
- The goal is to collate data into a manageable form and construct a **narrative** around it
- Use examples while keeping the narrative **concise and interesting**
- Show some numerical data — but only to **judge trends**, not to analyse every data point
- Write as if the reader may **not possess a lot of knowledge** of the subject
- Unlike a scientific study: case study is based on **opinion** and is designed to **provoke reasoned debate**
- There is **no definitive right or wrong answer** in a case study

> **Professor's note:** This is fundamentally different from quantitative research where you test a hypothesis and get a clear accept/reject answer. Case studies embrace ambiguity and multiple valid interpretations.
>
> *Điều này khác căn bản so với nghiên cứu định lượng. Nghiên cứu trường hợp chấp nhận sự mơ hồ và nhiều diễn giải hợp lệ.*

---

### Step 5: Write Up

Structure of a case study report:

1. **Introduce** the problem
2. Give **background** to the case study
3. **Present the information** you have discovered — use headings
4. **Evaluate and analyse** the information
5. **Summarise** findings in a conclusion — but remember, a case study may not always produce a neat, definitive conclusion

---

## 5. How to Analyse & Write Up Results

### Analysis Principles

| Principle | What it means |
|---|---|
| Opinion-based | No statistical hypothesis testing — judgement and interpretation dominate |
| Narrative construction | Build a coherent story from your data |
| Selective use of numbers | Show some numerical trends, but don't get lost in statistics |
| Accessible writing | Write for a non-expert reader |
| No single right answer | Multiple valid interpretations are acceptable and expected |

## 6. Limitations of Case Study

Six key limitations — must know for exams:

| # | Limitation | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Difficult to study objectively** | Researcher's personal views and biases easily influence interpretation |
| 2 | **Difficulty formulating hypotheses** | No clear hypothesis structure → findings are less testable |
| 3 | **Data not collected systematically** | Collection is flexible and evolving, not structured → harder to compare |
| 4 | **Statistical inferences cannot be drawn** | Small sample → cannot generalise to broader population statistically |
| 5 | **Time-consuming and costly** | Deep, contextual research takes far longer than a survey |
| 6 | **Does not contribute new knowledge** | Findings describe a specific case → may not add general theoretical knowledge |

> **The fundamental trade-off:** Case study sacrifices breadth (generalisability) and objectivity for depth (rich, contextual understanding). Whether that trade-off is worth it depends entirely on the research question.
>
> *Sự đánh đổi cơ bản: Nghiên cứu trường hợp hy sinh chiều rộng và tính khách quan để đổi lấy chiều sâu và sự phong phú ngữ cảnh.*

## 7. What is Research Ethics?

### Definition

> Research involves collecting information from or about people. Many people are willing to disclose **personal information** during research. We must treat both the **participants** and the **information they provide** with **honesty and respect**. This is research ethics.

*Đạo đức nghiên cứu: đối xử với cả người tham gia lẫn thông tin họ cung cấp bằng sự trung thực và tôn trọng.*

### Why Ethics Matters

Research is not neutral — it intrudes on people's lives:
- It takes their time
- It asks them to share personal, sometimes sensitive, information
- It may involve vulnerable individuals (children, the elderly, the ill)
- The information gathered could potentially harm participants if misused

---

## 8. Treating Participants with Respect

### Special Considerations

**Vulnerable participants** require extra care:

| Participant type | Required action |
|---|---|
| **Young/children** | A parent or guardian must be present |
| **Ill or elderly** | May need a proxy; ensure proxy relationship with participant is not damaged |
| **Powerless positions** | Extra sensitivity to power dynamics — participants should never feel coerced |

### Core Obligations

- **Minimise disruption** to people's lives
- If a participant found the experience upsetting → find out why → ensure it doesn't happen again
- **Ensure anonymity** — protect identity throughout data collection, analysis, and reporting
- **Ensure confidentiality** — protect the content of what participants shared

---

## 9. Code of Ethics — Four Pillars

A Code of Ethics is a formal document provided to participants **before** they take part — before a focus group, interview, or questionnaire. It explains what will be done with the information they provide and demonstrates respect and honesty.

*Bộ Quy tắc Đạo đức là tài liệu chính thức cung cấp cho người tham gia trước khi họ tham gia nghiên cứu.*

---

### Pillar 1: Anonymity

**What it means:** What participants say cannot be **traced back to them** in the final report.

**Practical questions to answer:**
- How will you categorise and store the information?
- How will you prevent access by people with bad intentions?
- Will you change the names of people, towns, and organisations?
- If you don't change names → how will you ensure what someone says cannot be used against them?
- **Critical warning:** Do not make promises about anonymity that you cannot keep.

*Ẩn danh: thông tin người tham gia cung cấp không thể truy nguyên về họ trong báo cáo cuối cùng.*

---

### Pillar 2: Confidentiality

**What it means:** Information supplied **in confidence** will not be disclosed to **third parties**.

**Key points:**
- In group settings (focus groups): confidentiality is relevant to the **entire group** — all members should agree not to disclose what was said
- Think carefully about how to categorise and store information so it cannot fall into others' hands
- Again: do not make promises you cannot keep

**Anonymity vs Confidentiality:**
| | Anonymity | Confidentiality |
|---|---|---|
| **Protects** | Who said it (identity) | What was said (content) |
| **Concern** | Traceability of statements | Disclosure to third parties |

---

### Pillar 3: Final Report

**What it means:** Participants have the right to know **what will happen with the results**.

**Address these questions:**
- Who will receive a free copy of the report?
- Will it be on public display?
- If the final report is very long → can you produce a shorter, more accessible summary to send to interested participants?

*Người tham gia có quyền biết điều gì sẽ xảy ra với kết quả nghiên cứu.*

---

### Pillar 4: Data Protection

**What it means:** Understand and comply with relevant **data protection laws**.

**Australian Privacy Act 1988:**
- Regulates how personal information is handled
- Administered by the Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC)
- URL: https://www.oaic.gov.au/privacy-law/privacy-act/

**Definition of personal information (from the Act):**
> "…information or an opinion, whether true or not, and whether recorded in a material form or not, about an identified individual, or an individual who is reasonably identifiable."

**Common examples of personal information:**
- Name, signature, address
- Telephone number, date of birth
- Medical records, bank account details
- Commentary or opinion about a person

*Thông tin cá nhân bao gồm: tên, địa chỉ, ngày sinh, hồ sơ y tế, chi tiết tài khoản ngân hàng, và bất kỳ ý kiến nào về một người.*

## 10. Ethical Guidelines & Institutional Framework

### Australian Research Council (ARC) Guidelines

The ARC has produced official **Codes of Ethics and Guidelines**:
- URL: http://www.arc.gov.au/codes-and-guidelines
- Covers: professional integrity; relations with and responsibilities towards research participants; relations with and responsibilities towards sponsors/funders
- A detailed reference for all ethical issues that may arise during research

### Academic Institutions

- All academic institutions have **ethics policies** for research
- Researchers must **identify ethical issues** and describe how they will be addressed
- Must consider ethical issues from the **respondents' viewpoint** — especially potential harm
- Must detail the **mechanism** in place to deal with any potential harm

### UOW Ethics Process (two steps)

| Step | Description | URL |
|---|---|---|
| **Policy** | Understanding UOW's research ethics policy | uow.edu.au/research/ethics/human/ |
| **Approval** | Submitting for ethics committee approval before research begins | uow.edu.au/research/ethics/human/ |

> **Professor's key point:** Ethics approval is not optional — it must be obtained BEFORE data collection begins with human participants. This is non-negotiable at any serious institution.
>
> *Phê duyệt đạo đức không phải tùy chọn — phải nhận được TRƯỚC khi bắt đầu thu thập dữ liệu từ người tham gia.*

```
├── 6 TYPES

```

```
│
├── CORE PRINCIPLE: Treat participants AND their information
```
