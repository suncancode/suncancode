## 1. What is Research?
Research is like joining an open international debate - you find the appropriate point to contribute and add your piece.

## 2. What is Science?
- **Science**: a systematic and organized body of knowledge accquired using the **science method**.
- **Purpose of science**: to create **scientific knowledge**.
- **Scienctific knowledge**: a generalized body of laws and theories that explain a phenomenon or behaviour.

### Branches of Science
**Natural Science** - science of naturally occurring objects or phenomena:
- Physical science
- Earth sciences
- Life sciences

**Social science** - science of people and their behaviours:
- Psychology (human beha)
- Sociology (social group)
- Economics (firms, markets, economies)

---

## 3. Scientific Knowledge 

### Components
- **Laws** - observed patterns of phenomena or behaviours.
- **Theories** - systematic explanations of the underlying phenomenon or behaviour.

### Two Pillars
- **Logic (Theory)** - provides meaning and significance to what we observe.
- **Evidence (Observation)** -helps validate, refine, or construct new theory.

### Example: Newtonian Laws of Motion
- **First Law** — what happens when an object is at rest or in motion
- **Second Law** — the force needed to move or stop an object
- **Third Law** — what happens when two objects collide

### Important Notes
- Scientific knowledge may be **imperfect** or far from the truth
- There may not be a single universal truth — rather an equilibrium of "multiple truths"
- Theories are explanations from **a particular perspective** and may be good or poor depending on how well they fit reality

## 4. Natural Science vs Social Science

| Aspect | Natural Science | Social Science |
|--------|----------------|----------------|
| Precision | Very precise and accurate | Less accurate, highly variable |
| Determinism | Deterministic (or probabilistic) | Non-deterministic, ambiguous |
| Objectivity | Independent of the observer | Subject to measurement error |
| Consensus | High agreement | Considerable uncertainty |

## 5. Two levels of Scientific Research

### Theoretical Level
- Developing **abstract concepts** about natural or social phenomena
- Building relationships between those concepts

### Empirical Level
- **Testing** theoretical concepts and relationships against real-world observations
- Goal: ultimately build **better theories**

## 6. Two forms of Scientific Research

### Deductive (Theory Testing)
- Testing concepts and patterns from existing theory using **new empirical data**
- Useful when there are **competing theories** and researchers want to know which works best

### Inductive (Theory building)
- Inferring theoretical concepts and patterns **from observed data**
- Valuable when there are **few prior theories** or explanations

### The Research Cycle

```
        THEORY
       ↗       ↘
  Induction   Deduction
       ↖       ↙
      OBSERVATIONS
```

## 7. Scientific Method

**Definition**: A standardized set of techniques for building scientific knowledge, covering:
- How to make **valid observations**
- How to **interpret results**
- How to **generalize** those results

### Key Properties
**1. Replicability**
> Others should be able to independently replicate or repeat a scientific stydy and obtain similar, if not identical, results.

**2. Precision**
> Theoretical concepts must be defined with such precision that others can use those definitions to measure those concepts and test that theory.

## 8. Steps in Conducting Research

A generic research process applicable to most research:
```
1. Choose a problem area (or topic)
        ↓
2. Review the published literature
        ↓
3. Define the research problem/question
        ↓
4. Develop a hypothesis [or objectives]
   → equivalent to developing a model of the problem
        ↓
5. Choose a method (or methods) to be employed
        ↓
6. Carry out the research
        ↓
7. Analyse data and benchmark against known results
        ↓
8. Write up results AND draw conclusions
   → Identify significance, insights, limitations & future research
        ↓
9. Publish or Perish
```

## 9. Topic Selection

### Selection Criteria
- Access to information and resources
- Theoretical background available 
- Value and significance of the research
- Researcher's own skills and expertise
- Is the question **big enought AND small enough**?
- Overall probability of successful completion
- Personal interest to researcher

### Important points
- Pick a topic **narrow enough** to be focused, but **not so narrow** that no information can be found
- You must **own the topic** once chosen. It is your topic, not your supervisor's.

## 10. Reviewing the Literature

>"This is perhaps the most important part in the early stages and during the research process.:

### Benefits of Literature Review
- **Mastery** of the research field, knowing what is important
- **Inspiration** to generate new ideas
- Ability to **find gaps** in existing literature
- Ability to **pose research questions**
- Knowing what findings to expect and how others measure their work
- **Avoiding reinvention of the wheel**

### How to Find Information
- Peer-reviewed **conference** and **journal** papers
- University library (e-library and physical library)
- Internet — *but be careful about internet junk!*

### How to Start
Begin by being organized: **collect**, **collate**, and **annotate** appropriate papers.

### Characteristics of a Good Literature Review
- Documents the **state of research** on a given topic
- Summarizes and **synthesizes** ideas, problems, and solutions over time
- Reflects the reviewer's **understanding** of the body of work
- May shed new light or provide **new interpretation** of previous works

## 11. Defining the Research Problem

- A set of **problem statements** is the output of a properly conducted literature review
- Research questions usually arise from **gaps identified** during the review
- The **quality of the questions** depends on the quality of the review

## 12. Research Papers & Publication

### What is a Research Paper?
- A **writeup of your research results**
- Written according to the **theme** of the research
- Written according to the **style** of the target journal or conference proceedings

### Publication Process
```
Research → Write Paper → Submit → Peer Review → Revise/Rebuttal → Acceptance → Final Submission → Publication
```

---

## 13. Structure of a Research Paper

### Overview

| Section | Category |
|---------|----------|
| Title, Authors, Abstract, Keywords | Front Matter |
| Introduction, Related Work, Preliminaries | Background |
| Method, Results, Analysis/Discussion, Conclusion | Main Body |
| References / Bibliography | Reference |

### Section-by-Section Breakdown

#### Title
- Captures the **research problem and solution**
- Tells the reader the **key content**
- Uses relevant **keywords** to capture the core of the paper

#### Abstract
Must cover: **background → research problem → solution → significance**

> Example: Clearly state the problem context, identify the challenge, propose your solution, and state the significance of results.

#### Keywords
- Most significant terms to help journals/conferences find suitable reviewers
- Help readers find relevant papers

#### Introduction
Must cover:
1. Background of research
2. Motivation
3. Research problem
4. Current methods or solutions
5. Why current methods cannot solve your problem
6. Your proposed method
7. Your result / contribution
8. Organisation of the paper

#### Related Work
- Select papers **most related** to your work
- Summarise their **key contributions and results**
- Comment on their **pros and cons**

#### Preliminaries
- Preparation for describing your method
- Provide notation, definitions, or foundational concepts needed to understand your approach

#### Presentation of Method and Result
- The **main body** of the paper
- Present the method: qualitative / quantitative / mathematical
- Present the result (format depends on method and research type)

#### Analysis or Discussion
- Convince readers the result is **correct, sound, and significant**
- May be based on simulation or experiment
- Include **comparison with other works**

#### Conclusion
- Highlight what you have done
- Briefly explain the **significance** of your result
- State the **problem solved** and **result achieved**
- Identify **future work** and open problems

#### References and Citation
- List all papers cited in the paper
- Must cite any paper related to the content (including your own prior work)
- **Never put citations in the abstract**

## 14. Submission & Peer Review

### Submission Types

| Type | Description | Common in |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| Blind submission | No author information in paper | Most conferences |
| Unblind submission | Full author information provided | Most journals |

### Peer Review
- Evaluation of work by people of **similar competence** in the field
- A form of **self-regulation** by qualified professionals
- Maintains **standards of quality**, improves performance, provides credibility
- Used to determine a paper's **suitability for publication**

### Revising a Paper
When asked to revise:
- Address **every constructive comment**
- Modify the paper accordingly
- **Never blame the reviewer**, even if comments seem incorrect
- If you disagree, use **polite and professional language**
- **Highlight changes** in the revised version
- Resubmit

### Final Submission
- Reformat the paper to match the journal/conference **style template**
- Submit final version with a **signed copyright form**

### Types of Publication
| Type | Access |
|------|--------|
| Paper-based | Subscription required to get a copy |
| Online (e.g., Springer, IEEE Xplore, ScienceDirect) | Subscription required to access database |
| Open source online | Publication fee charged; freely accessible to all |

## 15. Impact Factor & Citations

### Citation Metrics for Authors
- **Citation count** — total number of citations of all papers published by an author
- **h-index** — measures both **productivity** and **citation impact**
  - A researcher has h-index = *h* if *h* of their papers each have at least *h* citations

### Journal Impact Factor (Web of Science)
- **Journal IF** = average number of times articles published in the **past 2 years** have been cited in the JCR year
- **5-Year Journal IF** = same concept but calculated over **past 5 years**

> High impact factor = more influential journal in the field
