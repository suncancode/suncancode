# Project Risk Management

---

## 1. What is Risk?

### Definitions

> **PRINCE2:** *"The chance of exposure to the adverse consequences of future events."*

> **PMBOK:** *"An uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on a project's objectives."*

### Key Characteristics of Risk
- Risks relate to **possible future** problems — not current ones
- Every risk involves a **cause** and its **effect(s)**
- Risk management may also identify **opportunities** — unexpected positive events to be exploited

### Cause → Effect Examples

| Cause | Effect |
|---|---|
| Developer leaves the project | Task is delayed |
| Inaccurate estimate of effort | Cost over-run |
| Inexperienced staff | Testing takes longer than planned |
| Users uncertain of their requirements | Project scope increases |

---

## 2. Categories of Risk

Risks can be grouped into four main categories. A single risk may belong to **more than one** category.

| Category | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Actors** | Risks involving people — developers, users, managers | High staff turnover → project knowledge is lost |
| **Technology** | Risks from technology used to build or embedded in the project | Technologies selected are not appropriate |
| **Structures** | Risks from organizational structure and communication | Team members not informed of their responsibilities due to poor communication network |
| **Tasks** | Risks from the work itself | Effort needed for a task is underestimated |

> **Note:** Risks can also arise from **relationships between categories** — e.g., if the development technology is novel, developers may lack experience (Technology + Actors interaction).

---

## 3. Risk Management Framework

Risk management follows four steps:

```
Risk Identification → Risk Analysis → Risk Planning → Risk Monitoring
  (What risks?)      (How serious?)   (What to do?)   (Is it working?)
```

## 4. Risk Identification

### Approaches

| Approach | Description |
|---|---|
| **Checklists** | Based on experience from past projects — structured list of common risks |
| **Brainstorming** | Knowledgeable stakeholders gather to pool concerns |
| **Causal mapping** | Identifying possible chains of cause and effect |

### Boehm's Top 10 Software Risks

Barry Boehm surveyed software engineering project leaders to identify the most common risks:

| Risk | Risk Reduction Techniques |
|---|---|
| **Personnel shortfalls** | Staffing with top talent; job matching; team building; training; early scheduling of key personnel |
| **Unrealistic time and cost estimates** | Multiple estimation techniques; design to cost; incremental development; analysis of past projects; standardization of methods |
| **Developing the wrong software functions** | Improved software evaluation; formal specification; user surveys; prototyping; early user manuals |
| **Developing the wrong user interface** | Prototyping; task analysis; user involvement |
| **Gold plating** | Requirements scrubbing; prototyping; design to cost |
| **Late changes to requirements** | Change control; incremental development |
| **Shortfalls in externally supplied components** | Benchmarking; inspections; formal specifications; contractual agreements; quality controls |
| **Shortfalls in externally performed tasks** | Quality assurance procedures; competitive design |
| **Real-time performance problems** | Simulation; prototyping; tuning |
| **Development technically too difficult** | Technical analysis; cost-benefit analysis; prototyping; training |

> **Gold plating** = inclusion of features that are unnecessary and never actually used by anyone.

## 5. Risk Analysis and Prioritization

### Risk Exposure (RE) Formula

```
Risk Exposure (RE) = Potential Damage × Probability of Occurrence
```

- **Potential damage** — ideally expressed as a monetary value
- **Probability** — from 0.00 (impossible) to 1.00 (certain)

### Example
- A data centre is vulnerable to fire. If fire occurs → $500,000 recovery cost
- Estimated probability of fire = 1 in 100 = 0.01

```
RE = $500,000 × 0.01 = $5,000
```

This is analogous to an **insurance premium** — in a risk pooling arrangement, 100 companies each paying $5,000 would cover the 1-in-100 chance of fire.

### Qualitative Descriptors

**Probability Levels:**

| Level | Range |
|---|---|
| High | > 50% chance of happening |
| Significant | 30–50% chance |
| Moderate | 10–29% chance |
| Low | < 10% chance |

**Impact Levels:**

| Level | Range |
|---|---|
| High | > 30% above budgeted expenditure |
| Significant | 20–29% above budget |
| Moderate | 10–19% above budget |
| Low | Within 10% of budget |

### Probability-Impact Matrix

Used to **prioritize risks** visually:

```
         │  Low Impact  │  Moderate  │  High Impact
─────────┼──────────────┼────────────┼─────────────
High     │    Medium    │    High    │  Critical
Prob     │              │            │
─────────┼──────────────┼────────────┼─────────────
Moderate │     Low      │   Medium   │    High
Prob     │              │            │
─────────┼──────────────┼────────────┼─────────────
Low      │   Negligible │    Low     │   Medium
Prob     │              │            │
```

Example risks plotted on matrix:
- **R1**: Changes to requirements during coding
- **R2**: Specification takes longer than expected
- **R3**: Significant staff sickness affecting **critical path** activities
- **R4**: Significant staff sickness affecting **non-critical** path activities

## 6. Risk Planning — Strategies

### Three Main Strategies

| Strategy | Description | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Risk acceptance** | Acknowledge the risk and deal with consequences if it occurs | Low probability/impact risks |
| **Risk avoidance** | Change plans to eliminate the risk altogether | High impact risks that can be designed around |
| **Risk mitigation (reduction)** | Take actions to reduce probability or impact | Most common approach |

### Risk Reduction Leverage (RRL)

Used to assess **cost-effectiveness** of a risk reduction action:

```
RRL = (RE_before - RE_after) / Cost of risk reduction
```

**Example:**
- 1% chance of fire causing $200,000 damage
- RE_before = 1% × $200,000 = $2,000
- Fire alarm costing $500 reduces probability to 0.5%
- RE_after = 0.5% × $200,000 = $1,000

```
RRL = ($2,000 - $1,000) / $500 = 2.0
```

> **Rule:** If RRL > 1.0 → the action is **worth doing**

## 7. Risk Register

A **Risk Register** is a document that records all identified risks and tracks their status throughout the project.

Typical columns in a risk register:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Risk ID | Unique identifier |
| Risk description | What is the risk? |
| Category | Actor / Technology / Structure / Task |
| Probability | High / Significant / Moderate / Low |
| Impact | High / Significant / Moderate / Low |
| Risk Exposure (RE) | Probability × Damage |
| Mitigation action | What will be done |
| Owner | Who is responsible |
| Status | Open / Closed / Monitoring |

---

## 8. Risk Monitoring

The purpose of risk monitoring is to:
- Track identified risks and whether they have **occurred**
- Assess whether **mitigation actions** are working
- Identify **new risks** that emerge during the project
- Update the **Risk Register** regularly

## 9. PERT — Evaluating Schedule Risk

**Project Evaluation and Review Technique (PERT)** is a statistical tool that incorporates **uncertainty** into project scheduling.

### Three Time Estimates per Activity

| Estimate | Symbol | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Optimistic time** | a | Shortest realistic time — if everything goes better than normal |
| **Most likely time** | m | Expected time under normal conditions |
| **Pessimistic time** | b | Worst possible time — if everything goes wrong (excluding catastrophes) |

### PERT Formulas

**Expected time:**
```
te = (a + 4m + b) / 6
```

**Activity standard deviation:**
```
S = (b - a) / 6
```

### Chain of Activities — Example

| Task | a | m | b | te | S |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | 10 | 12 | 16 | 12.67 | 1.00 |
| B | 8 | 10 | 14 | 10.33 | 1.00 |
| C | 20 | 24 | 38 | 25.67 | 3.00 |

**Expected duration of A + B + C:**
```
te_total = 12.67 + 10.33 + 25.67 = 48.65 days
```

**Standard deviation of A + B + C:**
```
S_total = √(1² + 1² + 3²) = √11 = 3.32 days
```

### Assessing Probability of Meeting a Target

**Z-score formula:**
```
z = (T - te) / S
```

Where T = target completion date

**Example:** Target = 52 days
```
z = (52 - 48.65) / 3.32 = 1.01
```
→ Look up z = 1.01 in a standard normal table → approximately **84%** chance of meeting the target.

## 10. Critical Chain Approach

### The Problem with Traditional Estimates

When estimators plan tasks, they typically add a **safety buffer** to their estimate:
- Task estimated at 8 days → estimator adds 2 days safety → reported as 10 days
- Developers then work to the 10-day estimate (**Student Syndrome**) — taking the full 10 days even when it could have been done in 8

**Student Syndrome:** People tend to start activities **as late as possible**, leaving no buffer if problems arise.

**Result:** Safety zones are wasted. Early finishes are not passed forward. The project still runs late.

### Critical Chain Solution — 5 Steps

**Step 1:** Ask estimators for **two estimates**:
- **Most likely duration** — 50% chance of meeting this
- **Comfort zone** — additional time needed to have 95% chance

**Step 2:** Schedule all activities using **most likely values** with **latest start dates** (not earliest).

**Step 3:** Identify the **Critical Chain** — the longest chain of activities accounting for both:
- **Task dependencies** (activity B can't start until A finishes)
- **Resource dependencies** (activity C must wait for the same developer who is on activity A)

**Step 4:** Add a **Project Buffer** at the end of the critical chain:
```
Project Buffer = 50% × (sum of comfort zones on the critical chain)
```

**Step 5:** Where **subsidiary chains** feed into the critical chain, add **Feeding Buffers**:
```
Feeding Buffer = 50% × (sum of comfort zones of activities in the feeding chain)
```
Where there are parallel chains → use the **longest** comfort zone total.

### Executing the Critical Chain Plan

- No chain starts **earlier** than scheduled
- Once started, activities are completed **as soon as possible** — the **relay race principle**
- The next activity begins **immediately** when the current one finishes — even if early

### Buffer Monitoring — Traffic Light System

Buffers are divided into three zones for monitoring:

| Zone | Buffer Consumed | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 🟢 **Green** | First 33% | No action required |
| 🟡 **Amber** | Next 33% (34–66%) | Formulate an action plan |
| 🔴 **Red** | Last 33% (67–100%) | Execute the action plan immediately |
