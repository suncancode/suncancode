# Introduction to IT Project Management

---

## 1. Why is Project Management Important?

ICT receives massive government and corporate investment:
- The UK government spent £2.3 billion on ICT contracts in 2003–4 (vs. £1.4 billion on road building)
- The Australian government spent approximately $8 billion on ICT in 2022

Despite this, **IT projects frequently fail** (Standish Group):
- Only **1/3** of ICT projects are fully successful
- **82%** were delivered late
- **43%** exceeded their budget

> Poor project management is a major contributing factor to these failures.

---

## 2. What is a Project?

Common definitions:
- "A specific plan or design"
- "A planned undertaking"
- "A large undertaking, e.g. a public works scheme"

Key attributes: **planning** and **size/scope** of the task.

### Jobs vs. Projects vs. Exploration

| Type | Description | Uncertainty |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Jobs** | Repetition of well-defined, well-understood tasks | Very low |
| **Projects** | Planned undertakings with defined goals | Moderate |
| **Exploration** | Open-ended work (e.g. finding a cure for cancer) | Very high |

> Projects sit in the middle — more structured than exploration, less routine than jobs.

### Characteristics of a Project
A task is more "project-like" when it is:
- Non-routine
- Planned
- Aiming at a specific target
- Carried out for a customer
- Carried out by a temporary work group
- Involving several specialisms
- Made up of several different phases
- Constrained by time and resources
- Large and/or complex

## 3. Examples of IT Projects

- A team of students creates and sells a smartphone application

- A government group develops a child immunization tracking system
- A global bank consolidates systems after acquiring other financial institutions

**Gartner Top Strategic Technology Trends for 2026** include:

- Multiagent systems, Domain-specific language models, Physical AI
- Preemptive cybersecurity, Digital provenance, AI security platforms, Geopatriation

## 4. Are IT Project Different?

Not fundamentally different from other projects, but software has unique challenges:

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Invisibility** | Software progress is not physically visible (unlike a building) |
| **Complexity** | Software can be extremely complex relative to its size |
| **Conformity** | Must conform to the requirements of other systems/people |
| **Flexibility** | Ease of change leads to scope creep and instability |

## 5. In-house vs. Outsourced Projects

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **In-house** | Clients and developers belong to the same organisation |
| **Outsourced** | Clients and developers belong to different organisations |

In outsourced projects, the "project manager" role may be split between:
- A **contract manager** on the client side
- A **technical project manager** on the supplier/services side

## 6. Project Constraints — The Triple Constraint

Successful project management means meeting **all three goals** and satisfying the project sponsor:

```
         Target (sponsor satisfaction)
              ▲
              │
   Scope ─────┼───── Cost
              │
            Time
```

- **Scope goal** — deliver the agreed functionality
- **Time goal** — deliver on schedule
- **Cost goal** — deliver within budget

If one constraint is under pressure, it can be balanced by adjusting the others (the "project triangle").

---

## 7. What is Project Mangement?

>"The application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet project requirements."
> — *PMBOK® Guide, Sixth Edition, 2017*

Project managers strive to meet the **triple constraint** (scope, time, cost) while satisfying all stakeholder needs and expectations.

### 10 Knowledge Areas (PMBOK Framework)

**Core areas:**
- Scope management
- Schedule management
- Cost management
- Quality management

**Supporting areas:**
- Resource management
- Communications management
- Risk management
- Procurement management
- Stakeholder management

**Integrating all areas:**
- Project integration management

## 8. Activities Covered by Project Management

Three main phases:

| Phase | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Feasibility Study** | Is the project technically and organisationally feasible? Is it worthwhile from a business perspective? |
| **Planning** | Only undertaken if the project is deemed feasible — defines *how* it will be done |
| **Execution** | Implements the plan; the plan may be adapted as the project progresses |

---

## 9. Stakeholders

**Stakeholders** are persons or organisations that may be affected by a project, directly or indirectly.

They may include:
- Anyone who **operates** the system
- Anyone who **benefits** from the system
- Anyone involved in **purchasing** the system
- Organisations that **regulate** aspects of the system
- People or organisations **opposed** to the system
- Organisations responsible for **interfacing systems**

> Different stakeholders may have different objectives — a key task is defining **common project objectives**.

---

## 10. Project Success

A project can be considered successful if:
- It met **scope, time, and cost** goals
- It **satisfied the customer/sponsor**
- The results met the **main business objective** (e.g. ROI, savings, sponsor satisfaction)

### Setting Objectives
- Requires a **project authority** (project ownership) to set scope, allocate costs, and approve decisions
- Objective statement format: *"The project will be regarded as a success if…"*
  - Focus on **what** will be put in place, not **how** activities will be carried out

---

## 11. SMART Objectives

Objectives should be:

| Letter | Meaning | Example Issue |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| **S** – Specific | Concrete and well-defined | "Improve customer satisfaction" is too vague |
| **M** – Measurable | Can be objectively judged | "Reduce customer complaints" needs a number |
| **A** – Achievable | Within the power of the team | "Send humans to Mars" may not be realistic |
| **R** – Relevant | Aligned with the true purpose of the project | Must connect to actual business goals |
| **T** – Time-constrained | Has a defined deadline | Must specify a target date |

**Good example:** *"Upgrade the helpdesk telephone system by December 31 to achieve average client wait times of no more than two minutes."*

### Sub-objectives
Steps along the way to achieving the main objective:
> *"To reach objective X, the following must be in place: A… B… C…"*

---

## 12. Measures of Effectiveness

How do we know an objective has been achieved? Through **practical, objectively assessable tests**.

Examples for user satisfaction:
- Repeat business (customers buy further products)
- Number of complaints received

---

## 13. The Business Case

A project is justified when:

```
Benefits of delivered project > Costs
```

**Costs include:**
- Development costs
- Operational costs

**Benefits can be:**
- Quantifiable (measurable in dollars)
- Non-quantifiable (e.g. brand reputation, staff morale)

---

## 14. Project Success vs. Failure

### Project Objectives
The project team is expected to deliver:
- The agreed **functionality** (scope)
- The required level of **quality**
- **On time**
- **Within budget**

### Balancing the Triangle
- Running out of time → recover by **reducing scope** or **increasing cost**
- Over budget → recover by **adjusting scope** or **extending time**

### Other (Long-term) Success Criteria
- Improved skill and knowledge in the team
- Creation of reusable assets (e.g. software libraries)
- Improved customer relationships leading to repeat business

---

## 15. What is Management?

Management involves 8 key activities:
1. **Planning**: Deciding what is to be done
2. **Organising**: Making arrangements
3. **Staffing**: Selecting the right people for the job
4. **Directing**: Giving instruction
5. **Monitoring**: Checking on progress
6. **Controlling**: Taking action to remedy holdups
7. **Innovating**: Coming up with solutions when problems emerge
8. **Representing**: Liaising with clients, users, developers, and stakeholders

## 16. Management Control

The control cycle involves:

```
                                Real World
                                    │
                                    ▼
                                Data Collection  
                                    │
                                    ▼
                                Data Processing
                                    │
                                Information
                                    |
                                    ▼
Define Objectives ──►  Making Decisions/Plan ◄── Modelling
                                    |
                                Decisions
                                    |
                                    ▼
                            Implementation ──► Actions ──► (back to Real World)
```

- **Data**: Raw details
- **Information**: Processed data that is meaningful
- **Comparison**: Measured against objectives/goals
- **Modelling**: Working out probable outcomes of decisions
- **Implementation**: Carrying out the remedial actions decided
