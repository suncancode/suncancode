# Project Stakeholder Management & Communications

---

## 1. Why Communication Matters

> *"The greatest threat to many projects is a failure to communicate."*

- Project managers spend up to **90% of their time communicating**
- IT professionals are often stereotyped as poor communicators — but research shows communication skills are **critical for career advancement**
- Strong verbal and non-technical skills are a **key factor** in IT career success
- A 2014 study found the most important non-technical skills are: **problem solving, teamwork, and listening**
- Some organizations hire people with **minimal technical skills** if they demonstrate solid soft and business skills

## 3. Keys to Good Communications

| Principle | Details |
|---|---|
| **Focus on individual + group needs** | People are not interchangeable — each has different communication styles |
| **Use formal AND informal methods** | Meetings, reports, casual conversations — all have a role |
| **Distribute information effectively and timely** | Right information, right people, right time |
| **Set the stage for bad news** | Create a culture where bad news is reported early |
| **Manage communication channels** | More people = exponentially more complexity |

## 4. Understanding Individual Communication Needs

### Brooks' Law — The Mythical Man-Month
> *"You cannot assume that a task originally scheduled to take two months of one person's time can be done in one month by two people."*

Adding more people to a late project often **makes it later** because:
- New team members need time to onboard
- Communication channels increase exponentially
- Productivity of existing members drops while they train newcomers

### Personal Preferences Affect Communication

| Preference Pair | One Type | Other Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Introvert vs Extrovert** | Prefers private, one-on-one communication | Prefers public discussion |
| **Intuitive vs Sensing** | Wants the big picture | Needs step-by-step details |
| **Thinking vs Feeling** | Wants logic behind decisions | Wants to know personal impact |
| **Judging vs Perceiving** | Driven to meet deadlines | Needs help developing and following plans |

> 💡 **Practical tip:** Project managers must adapt their communication style to the individual — there is no one-size-fits-all approach.

## 5. Face-to-Face Communication

Research on face-to-face interaction:

```
Body language     58%  ████████████████████████████
Tone of voice     35%  ████████████████
Actual words       7%  ████

Total             100%
```

**Key insight:** Only **7% of communication** is in the actual words. The other **93%** is non-verbal — tone, posture, eye contact, facial expressions.

> Always pay attention to **more than just the words** being said.

### Encouraging More Face-to-Face Interaction
- **Short, frequent meetings** are often very effective in IT projects
- **Stand-up meetings** force people to focus on what really needs to be communicated
- Some companies have policies preventing email between certain hours or on certain days of the week

## 6. Distributing Information Effectively

| Rule | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Don't bury crucial information** | Important information must be visible, not hidden in long documents |
| **Don't be afraid to report bad news** | Early bad news = time to fix it. Late bad news = crisis |
| **Use oral communication** | Meetings and informal talks bring important information — good and bad — out into the open |

### Other Communication Considerations
- Messages are **rarely interpreted exactly** as the sender intended
- **Geographic and cultural differences** add complexity:
  - Different working hours
  - Language barriers
  - Different cultural norms (e.g., directness, hierarchy, formality)

## 7. Number of Communication Channels

As team size increases, communication complexity increases **exponentially**.

### Formula

```
Number of channels = n(n - 1) / 2

Where n = number of people involved
```

### Examples

| Team Size (n) | Channels |
|---|---|
| 2 people | 2(1)/2 = **1** channel |
| 3 people | 3(2)/2 = **3** channels |
| 5 people | 5(4)/2 = **10** channels |
| 10 people | 10(9)/2 = **45** channels |
| 20 people | 20(19)/2 = **190** channels |

> 💡 This is why small teams communicate better — and why adding people to a project dramatically increases coordination overhead.

## 8. Planning Communications Management

Every project should have a **Communications Management Plan** — a document that guides project communications.

- **Small projects:** Can be part of the team contract
- **Large projects:** Should be a separate formal document

### Communications Management Plan — 8 Contents

| # | Content |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Stakeholder communications requirements** — who needs what |
| 2 | **Information to be communicated** — format, content, level of detail |
| 3 | **Who receives and who produces** each type of information |
| 4 | **Suggested methods or technologies** for conveying information |
| 5 | **Frequency** of communication |
| 6 | **Escalation procedures** for resolving issues |
| 7 | **Revision procedures** for updating the plan itself |
| 8 | **Glossary** of common terminology |

## 9. Managing Communications

Getting project information to the **right people**, at the **right time**, in a **useful format** is just as important as developing the information in the first place.

### Three Classifications of Communication Methods

| Method | Description | Effectiveness | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Interactive** | Two or more people exchange information in real time | **Most effective** — ensures common understanding | Meetings, phone calls, video conferencing |
| **Push** | Information sent to recipients without their request | Ensures distribution but **not** that it was received or understood | Reports, emails, faxes, voicemails |
| **Pull** | Information available for recipients to access when they want | Flexible, but depends on recipient initiative | Websites, bulletin boards, knowledge bases, blogs |

### Choosing the Right Method — Media Choice Considerations

| Situation | Recommended Method |
|---|---|
| Complex, sensitive, or ambiguous topic | Interactive (face-to-face) |
| Routine updates, status reports | Push (email, reports) |
| Reference documents, FAQs | Pull (wiki, portal) |
| Urgent, time-sensitive | Interactive or Push |
| Large audience, one-way | Push |

## 10. Performance Reporting

Performance reporting keeps stakeholders informed about **how resources are being used** to achieve project objectives.

| Report Type | Description | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| **Status reports** | Where the project stands at a **specific point in time** | Snapshot — e.g., "as of today" |
| **Progress reports** | What the team has **accomplished** during a certain period | Looking backward over a period |
| **Forecasts** | **Predictions** about future project status based on past information and trends | Looking forward |

---

## 11. Controlling Communications

**Goal:** Ensure the **optimal flow of information** throughout the entire project lifecycle.

The project manager and team should:
- Use a **change control system** to manage changes to project documents
- Conduct **regular meetings** to review project status
- Use **lessons learned** to improve communications
- Adjust the communications management plan if it is not working

## 13. Email Best Practices

| Guideline |
|---|
| Send email to the **right people** — use CC sparingly |
| Use **meaningful subject lines** |
| Limit content to **one main subject** per email |
| Make the body **as clear as possible** |
| **Always read before sending** |
| Limit number and size of **attachments** |
| Delete email you **don't need to save** |
| **Never open attachments** from untrusted sources |
| **Respond quickly** |
| **File each message** appropriately |

## 14. Using Templates

- Many technical people are reluctant to ask for help with communication
- Templates **save time and money** and ensure consistency
- Research shows companies that excel in project management make **effective use of templates**
- Common templates: Project Description, Monthly Progress Report, Lessons Learned Report

## 15. Lessons Learned Reports and Project Archives

### Lessons Learned Reports
- Each team member prepares a **reflective statement** documenting important things learned from the project
- The project manager combines these into a **project summary report**
- Invaluable for improving future projects

### Project Archives
- A **complete set of organized project records** providing an accurate history of the project
- Can include: plans, status reports, meeting minutes, change logs, contracts, final deliverables
- Provides **valuable reference material** for future similar projects

## 16. Project Stakeholder Management

### Why Stakeholder Management Matters
- PMI created an entire knowledge area devoted to stakeholder management in **2013** — recognizing its critical importance
- Projects often cause **organizational changes** — some people may lose their jobs
- Project managers can be seen as **enemies** (if jobs are lost) or **allies** (if profits increase or new jobs are created)
- Project managers must learn to **identify, understand, and work with** a wide variety of stakeholders

### Definition
> *"The purpose of project stakeholder management is to identify all people or organizations affected by a project, to analyze stakeholder expectations, and to effectively engage stakeholders."*

## 18. Identifying Stakeholders

### Internal vs External Stakeholders

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Internal** | Project sponsor, project team, support staff, internal customers, top management, functional managers, other project managers |
| **External** | External customers, competitors, suppliers, government officials, concerned citizens, user groups |

### Stakeholder Register

A **stakeholder register** documents key information about each stakeholder:

| Section | Contents |
|---|---|
| **Identification** | Name, position, location, role in project, contact information |
| **Assessment** | Major requirements, expectations, potential influences, phases of highest interest |
| **Classification** | Internal/external? Supporter/resistant? |

### Power/Interest Grid

Used to **classify stakeholders** and determine management approach:

```
        High │  Keep Satisfied  │  Manage Closely  │
             │                  │  (Highest effort) │
Power        ├──────────────────┼──────────────────┤
             │  Monitor         │  Keep Informed   │
        Low  │  (Minimal effort)│                  │
             └──────────────────┴──────────────────┘
                      Low                High
                            Interest
```

| Quadrant | Strategy |
|---|---|
| **High Power, High Interest** | Manage closely — most important group |
| **High Power, Low Interest** | Keep satisfied — they can block the project |
| **Low Power, High Interest** | Keep informed — they care and can be advocates |
| **Low Power, Low Interest** | Monitor — minimal effort needed |

### Stakeholder Engagement Levels

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Unaware** | Unaware of the project and its potential impacts |
| **Resistant** | Aware of the project but resistant to change |
| **Neutral** | Aware but neither supportive nor resistant |
| **Supportive** | Aware and supportive of change |
| **Leading** | Actively engaged in helping the project succeed |

> **Goal:** Move stakeholders from Unaware/Resistant → Supportive/Leading

---

## 19. Planning Stakeholder Management

After identifying and analyzing stakeholders, develop a **stakeholder management plan**.

### Contents of Stakeholder Management Plan

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **Current and desired engagement levels** | Where each stakeholder is now vs where they need to be |
| **Interrelationships between stakeholders** | Which stakeholders influence each other |
| **Communication requirements** | How and when to communicate with each |
| **Management strategies** | Specific approach for each stakeholder |
| **Methods for updating the plan** | How to keep the plan current |

### Confidentiality Note
> ⚠️ Because a stakeholder management plan often contains **sensitive information** (e.g., strategies for managing resistant stakeholders), it should **NOT** be part of official project documents available to all stakeholders.

- Only project managers and a few selected team members should prepare it
- Parts may not be written down at all
- If written, distribution is **strictly limited**

## 20. Managing Stakeholder Engagement

### Expectations Management Matrix
Project sponsors rank scope, time, and cost goals in order of importance — this ranking is captured in an **expectations management matrix** to clarify priorities and help manage trade-offs.

### Issue Log
Issues that arise must be documented and tracked:

| Issue Log Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Issue ID | Unique identifier |
| Description | What the issue is |
| Priority | High/Medium/Low |
| Owner | Who is responsible for resolving it |
| Status | Open/In Progress/Resolved |
| Target resolution date | Deadline for resolution |

> Unresolved issues are a **major source of conflict** and can result in stakeholder expectations not being met.

---

## 21. Monitoring Stakeholder Engagement

> *"You cannot control stakeholders, but you can control their level of engagement."*

- Engagement involves a **dialogue** — people seeking understanding and solutions to issues of mutual concern
- Monitor whether stakeholders are becoming more or less engaged over time
- Adjust communication strategies based on changes in stakeholder attitudes
- Use the **stakeholder register** and **engagement level assessments** to track progress
