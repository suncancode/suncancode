# Agile: Principles and Practices of Agile Project Management

---

## 1. What is a Software Development Methodology?

**Software Development Methodology** is a structured, systematic approach to managing the creation and maintenance of software. It provides a framework of processes, methods, and tools that a team uses throughout the software lifecycle.

Examples: Waterfall, DevOps, Agile-based methods (Scrum, Kanban...)

---

## 2. What is Agile?

- **Literal meaning:** The ability to move quickly and flexibly
- **Agile is NOT** a specific software development methodology
- Agile is a **philosophy and overarching approach** to delivering value to customers
- Typically implemented through **iterative development**
- Contrasts with the **Waterfall** model (linear, one-directional)

> Agile is a **guiding philosophy**, not a specific instruction manual. Teams decide how to implement it based on Agile values and principles.

---

## 3. History

### Problems of the 1990s

The IT industry faced significant frustration:
- Most projects followed the **Waterfall** model
- A **large time gap** between initial requirements and delivered product → many projects cancelled
- **Rapidly changing business environments** → final products no longer met actual needs
- Waterfall **failed to leverage** the fast-changing nature of software

### Birth of the Agile Manifesto

February 2001: **17 software experts** gathered at **Snowbird Ski Resort, Utah, USA** → created the **Agile Manifesto**.

---

## 4. Agile Manifesto — 4 Core Values

> *"We value the items on the left more than the items on the right — however, the items on the right still have value."*

| Higher Priority (Left) | | Lower Priority (Right) |
|---|---|---|
| **Individuals and interactions** | over | Process and tools |
| **Working software** | over | Comprehensive documentation |
| **Customer collaboration** | over | Contract negotiation |
| **Responding to change** | over | Following a plan |

### Explanation of Each Value

**1. Individuals & Interactions > Process & Tools**
- People and their interactions are the key factor in high-performing teams
- **Inspect-and-adapt cycles:**
  - Pair programming (every minute)
  - Continuous integration (every few hours)
  - Stand-up meeting (daily)
  - Review & retrospective meeting (every iteration)
- Tools and processes are still important but must serve the team, not the other way around

**2. Working Software > Comprehensive Documentation**
- Documentation matters but **working software matters more**
- Deliver small pieces of working software to customers **regularly** in each cycle

**3. Customer Collaboration > Contract Negotiation**
- Customers must be **involved and collaborating** throughout the development process
- There should be a **customer representative** on the team
- Purpose: ensure the product meets actual business needs

**4. Responding to Change > Following a Plan**
- Over **60%** of product/project requirements change during development
- Plans and processes must be **flexible** to accommodate change and customer feedback
- To succeed: **you must plan for change**

## 5. Agile Manifesto — 12 Principles

### Group 1: Delivering to Customers

| # | Principle |
|---|---|
| 1 | Highest priority is **satisfying the customer** through **early and continuous** delivery of valuable software |
| 2 | **Welcome changing requirements**, even late in development — Agile harnesses change for the customer's competitive advantage |
| 3 | **Deliver working software frequently** — from a couple of weeks to a couple of months, with a preference for shorter timescales |

### Group 2: People and Teams

| # | Principle |
|---|---|
| 4 | **Business people and developers must work together daily** throughout the project |
| 5 | Build projects around **motivated individuals**. Give them the environment and support they need, and trust them to get the job done |
| 6 | The most efficient and effective method of conveying information is **face-to-face conversation** |

### Group 3: Quality and Technical Excellence

| # | Principle |
|---|---|
| 7 | **Working software** is the primary measure of progress |
| 8 | Agile promotes **sustainable development** — sponsors, developers, and users should be able to maintain a constant pace indefinitely |
| 9 | Continuous attention to **technical excellence and good design** enhances agility |

### Group 4: Continuous Improvement

| # | Principle |
|---|---|
| 10 | **Simplicity** — the art of maximizing the amount of work not done — is essential |
| 11 | The best **architectures, requirements, and designs** emerge from self-organizing teams |
| 12 | At regular intervals, the team **reflects on how to become more effective**, then adjusts its behavior accordingly |

## 6. Popular Agile Methods

| Method | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Scrum** | Sprint-based, Product Backlog, Daily Standup (most popular) |
| **Extreme Programming (XP)** | Pair programming, TDD, continuous integration |
| **Kanban** | Visualize workflow, limit WIP (Work In Progress) |
| **Lean Software Development** | Eliminate waste, optimize the entire process |
| **Dynamic Systems Development Method (DSDM)** | RAD-based, timeboxing |

---

## 7. Agile vs Waterfall — Comparison

| Criteria | Waterfall | Agile |
|---|---|---|
| Approach | Linear, step-by-step | Iterative, flexible |
| Requirements | Fixed from the start | Can change at any time |
| Product delivery | Once at the end of the project | Continuously per iteration |
| Customer involvement | At the beginning and end | Throughout the entire process |
| Documentation | Very detailed and extensive | Just enough, prioritizes working software |
| Best suited for | Projects with clear, stable requirements | Projects with continuously changing requirements |

---

## 8. Common Misconceptions About Agile

| Misconception | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Agile = no planning" | Agile still plans, but the plan is flexible and adaptive |
| "Agile = no documentation" | Documentation still exists, but at a just-enough level — not excessive |
| "Non-Agile = poor management" | Some projects **must** use non-Agile (aviation, healthcare, construction...) |
| "Agile = no customer contracts" | Contracts still exist, but collaboration is prioritized over rigid contract negotiation |
| "Agile is a methodology" | Agile is a **philosophy** — Scrum and Kanban are the methodologies |

| Question | Answer | Explanation |

| 2 | **B (False)** | Agile does not say tools & processes are bad — it only says individuals & interactions are prioritized over them |
| 3 | **A (True)** | Agile emphasizes empowered teams to encourage innovation |
| 4 | **A (True)** | Too much documentation can hinder normal communication |
| 5 | **B (False)** | Contracts can still exist in an Agile environment |
| 6 | **B (False)** | Agile does not necessarily require rigidly defined requirements — requirements can change |
| 7 | **A (True)** | Agile optimizes product value through the ability to respond to change |
| 8 | **A (True)** | The 4 Agile values are relative, not absolute — situational judgment is required |
| 9 | **C** | Fixed-price bid on RFP → relates to "Customer collaboration over contract negotiation" |
| 10 | **D** | Customer realizes the solution is unsuitable midway → "Responding to change over following a plan" |
