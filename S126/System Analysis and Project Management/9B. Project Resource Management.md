# Project Resource Management

---

## 1. Why Human Resource Management Matters

> *"The most important asset in any organization is its people."*

People determine the **success and failure** of both organizations and projects. Unlike hardware or software, people bring creativity, judgment, motivation, and relationships — none of which can be automated or purchased off a shelf.

### Why It Is One of the Toughest Challenges
- People have **emotions, motivations, and personal goals** that may not align with project goals
- Team dynamics are **complex and unpredictable**
- Skills, availability, and performance vary widely between individuals
- Conflicts are inevitable when people work under pressure and tight deadlines
- Remote/virtual teams add communication and coordination complexity
- Organizational politics can interfere with rational resource decisions

---

## 2. What Motivates People?

Research (Daniel Pink, *Drive*) suggests that for knowledge workers, traditional monetary rewards have **limited long-term motivational effect**. The three most powerful intrinsic motivators are:

| Motivator | Description |
|---|---|
| **Autonomy** | The desire to direct your own work — to have control over what, when, how, and with whom you work |
| **Mastery** | The urge to get better at something that matters — continuous improvement and skill growth |
| **Purpose** | The sense that your work contributes to something larger than yourself |

> 💡 **Key insight for managers:** Focus on creating conditions for autonomy, mastery, and purpose — not just on salary and bonuses. Money matters up to a threshold, but beyond that, intrinsic motivation drives performance.

## 4. Developing the Human Resource Plan

Involves **identifying and documenting** project roles, responsibilities, and reporting relationships.

Key outputs of HR planning:

| Output | Description |
|---|---|
| **Project Organizational Chart** | Visual diagram showing reporting relationships and team structure |
| **Staffing Management Plan** | Describes when and how people will be added to and removed from the team |
| **Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RAM)** | Maps project work (from WBS) to the people responsible |
| **Resource Histogram** | Column chart showing number of resources assigned over time |

---

### 4.1 Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)

The **WBS** breaks the project into manageable tasks with time estimates and deliverables.

**Example — Analysis Phase:**

| Task | Sub-task | Duration | Deliverable |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Analysis | 1.1 Write Questionnaires | 3 days | Questionnaire |
| | 1.2 Conduct Interviews | 3 days | Interview Report |
| | 1.3 Develop SRS | 5 days | SRS Document Draft |
| | 1.4 Review SRS | 2 days | Reviewed and Approved SRS |

The WBS is the **foundation** for all other resource planning tools — you cannot assign people or create histograms without first knowing what work needs to be done.

---

### 4.2 Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RAM)

A **RAM** maps the work of the project (from WBS) to the people responsible (from the Organizational Breakdown Structure / OBS).

**Common format — RACI Chart:**

| Task | Project Manager | Developer | Tester | Client |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Write Requirements | A | R | C | I |
| Design Database | I | R | C | - |
| Code Module | I | R | - | - |
| User Acceptance Testing | A | C | R | I |

**RACI Definitions:**

| Letter | Role | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **R** | Responsible | Does the work |
| **A** | Accountable | Owns the outcome — only ONE per task |
| **C** | Consulted | Provides input/expertise — two-way communication |
| **I** | Informed | Kept up to date — one-way communication |

---

### 4.3 Staffing Management Plan

Describes **when and how** people will be **added to** and **taken off** the project team.

Key elements:
- Staff acquisition strategy (internal hire, external contract, outsource)
- Timeline for when each role is needed
- Training requirements
- Release criteria — when and how people leave the project
- Compliance and HR policies

## 5. Resource Loading and Leveling

### 5.1 Resource Loading

**Resource loading** = the amount of individual resources an existing schedule requires during specific time periods.

- Helps project managers understand **demands** a project will place on the organization
- Identifies **overallocation** — when more resources are assigned than are available at a given time
- An overallocated resource histogram shows a column **exceeding the available capacity line**

### 5.2 Resource Leveling

**Resource leveling** = a technique for resolving resource conflicts by **delaying tasks** to smooth out resource usage.

**Goal:** Create a smoother distribution of resource usage and reduce overallocation.

**How it works:**
- Identify tasks with **float/slack** (tasks that can be delayed without affecting the project end date)
- Delay those tasks to periods when the resource is less loaded
- Accept that the overall project schedule may extend slightly

## 6. Managing the Project Team

Project managers must **lead their teams** in performing project activities and continuously assess performance.

After assessing team performance, the project manager must decide:
- Whether **changes to the project** should be requested
- Whether **corrective or preventive actions** should be recommended
- Whether **updates** are needed to the project management plan or organizational process assets

### Tools and Techniques for Managing Project Teams

| Tool | Description |
|---|---|
| **Observation and conversation** | Direct, informal engagement with team members — often reveals issues early |
| **Project performance appraisals** | Formal assessment of individual and team performance against goals |
| **Interpersonal skills** | Emotional intelligence, listening, influence, negotiation |
| **Conflict management** | Structured approaches to resolving disagreements |
| **Risk management** | Proactively managing threats to team performance |

## 7. Conflict Management

### Why Conflict Happens in Projects
- Scarce resources shared between tasks or projects
- Schedule pressure and unrealistic deadlines
- Different priorities between team members
- Personality differences
- Unclear roles and responsibilities

### Conflict Is Not Always Bad

> *"Conflict often produces important results: new ideas, better alternatives, and motivation to work harder and more collaboratively."*

| Type of Conflict | Effect on Team |
|---|---|
| **Task-related conflict** | Often **improves** team performance — pushes for better solutions |
| **Emotional/personal conflict** | Often **depresses** team performance — creates dysfunction |

**Groupthink** — the danger of *no* conflict: when everyone conforms to group values without questioning, bad decisions go unchallenged. Healthy conflict prevents groupthink.

### Six Conflict Handling Modes

| Mode | Description | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Confrontation** | Directly face the conflict using a problem-solving approach | Best default — addresses root cause |
| **Compromise** | Give-and-take; both parties give up something | When both parties have equal power and time is limited |
| **Smoothing** | De-emphasize differences, emphasize agreement | Short-term relief; doesn't solve underlying issue |
| **Forcing** | Win-lose approach; one party imposes their solution | Emergency situations; unpopular but necessary decisions |
| **Withdrawal** | Retreat from the conflict | When the issue is trivial or when cooling down is needed |
| **Collaborating** | Incorporate different viewpoints; build consensus | When long-term relationships and buy-in matter most |

> 💡 **Best practice:** Confrontation (problem-solving) is generally considered the **most effective** long-term approach. Forcing and withdrawal are typically least effective for team health.

## 8. Building High-Performance Teams

### The 12 Factors of a High-Performance Team

| Factor | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Shared vision** | Clear, challenging, but achievable objective — creates pride and motivation |
| **Team identity** | A name, shared vocabulary, inside jokes, social bonds — "we" not "me" |
| **Results-driven structure** | Clear roles, accountability, regular reporting and code walkthroughs |
| **Competent team members** | Technical skills + social/collaboration skills |
| **Commitment to the team** | Personal sacrifice; subordinating individual goals to team goals |
| **Mutual trust** | Believing teammates will do their part |
| **Interdependence** | Recognizing and utilizing each member's relative strengths |
| **Effective communication** | Raising problems early rather than hoping they go away |
| **Sense of autonomy** | Team controls how work is done |
| **Sense of empowerment** | Team has authority to make decisions |
| **Small team size** | Optimal: 3–7 members (5–9 in Scrum) |
| **High level of enjoyment** | People who enjoy working together work better |

## 9. The Five Dysfunctions of a Team

Patrick Lencioni identifies five dysfunctions that prevent teams from achieving their potential. They build on each other like a pyramid:

```
               ┌─────────────────────────────┐
               │  5. Inattention to Results  │  ← Focus on ego/status over collective goals
               ├─────────────────────────────┤
               │  4. Avoidance of            │  ← Not holding each other accountable
               │     Accountability          │
               ├─────────────────────────────┤
               │  3. Lack of Commitment      │  ← Ambiguity; no buy-in to decisions
               ├─────────────────────────────┤
               │  2. Fear of Conflict        │  ← Artificial harmony; no passionate debate
               ├─────────────────────────────┤
               │  1. Absence of Trust        │  ← Fear of being vulnerable with teammates
               └─────────────────────────────┘
                        Foundation
```

| Dysfunction | Manifestation | Remedy |
|---|---|---|
| **Absence of trust** | Unwilling to be vulnerable; hide weaknesses | Team-building; personal history exercises |
| **Fear of conflict** | Artificial harmony; avoid difficult conversations | Encourage healthy debate; mining for conflict |
| **Lack of commitment** | Ambiguity about decisions and direction | Clarity; ensure everyone is heard before deciding |
| **Avoidance of accountability** | No peer pressure to improve performance | Publish goals; regular team reviews |
| **Inattention to results** | Ego and status over collective outcomes | Focus on team scoreboard; reward team results |

## 10. General Advice on Teams

### Building and Maintaining the Team
- Be **patient and kind** — fix problems, don't blame people
- Establish **regular, effective meetings** with a clear agenda
- Allow time for teams to go through **team-building stages** (Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing)
- Keep work teams **small**: 3–7 members is optimal
- Plan **social activities** to help members get to know each other
- **Stress team identity** — shared name, practices, and culture
- **Nurture team members** and encourage them to help each other
- Take additional actions for **virtual team members** who may feel isolated

### Tuckman's Team Development Stages

| Stage | What Happens |
|---|---|
| **Forming** | Team meets; polite, uncertain about roles and goals |
| **Storming** | Conflict emerges; members assert themselves; power struggles |
| **Norming** | Norms established; trust builds; collaboration improves |
| **Performing** | Team is fully functional; high performance; autonomous |
| **Adjourning** | Project ends; team disbands; reflection on achievements |

## 11. Effective Meetings

Much of the development process is **group-based** — brainstorming, planning, reviewing. Good meetings require structure.

### Standard Meeting Structure

| Phase | Activity |
|---|---|
| **Opening** | Review action items from previous meeting |
| **Main work** | Agenda items — discussion, decisions, problem-solving |
| **Closing** | Identify action items for next meeting; assign owners and deadlines |
| **After meeting** | Distribute **meeting minutes** promptly |

### Meeting Best Practices
- Distribute **agenda in advance** so participants can prepare
- Start and **end on time**
- Keep discussions **on topic** — use a "parking lot" for off-topic items
- Assign a **facilitator** (keeps discussion moving) and a **note-taker**
- Every action item must have an **owner** and a **due date**
- Send **meeting minutes** within 24 hours
