# Information Gathering, Modelling and Workflows

---

## 1. Introduction and Outline

The process of **Information Gathering, Modelling and Workflows** is a foundational phase in systems analysis and design. It involves:

1. **Collecting accurate and complete information** from stakeholders.
2. **Creating models** to represent the system.
3. **Documenting workflows** clearly using standard notations (especially UML Activity Diagrams).

**Lecture Outline**
- Information-Gathering Techniques
- Models and Modelling
- Workflow Documentation using Activity Diagrams

## 2. Information-Gathering Techniques

### 2.1 Interviewing Users and Other Stakeholders

**Purpose**: The most important and widely used technique to gather detailed information.

#### Key Steps

**Preparing the Interview**
- Establish the objective
- Determine who should be interviewed (identify every type of stakeholder)
- Compile detailed questions
- Make final arrangements and communicate to participants

**Conducting the Interview**
- Plan ahead and arrive early
- Manage time effectively
- Look for exceptions and error conditions (ask “what if” questions)
- Probe for details
- Take thorough notes

**Following Up the Interview**
- Review notes for accuracy and completeness
- Transfer information to models/documents
- Identify areas needing further clarification
- Thank participants
- Schedule follow-up meetings

#### Themes for Information-Gathering Questions

| Theme                                      | Questions to Users                                      |
|--------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| What are the business operations and processes? | What do you do?                                        |
| How should those operations be performed?  | How do you do it?<br>What steps do you follow?<br>How could they be done differently? |
| What information is needed to perform those operations? | What information do you use?<br>What inputs do you use?<br>What outputs do you produce? |

#### Question Types
- **Open-ended questions** → Encourage discussion and explanation (e.g., “How do you do this function?”)
- **Closed-ended questions** → Obtain specific facts (e.g., “How many forms a day do you process?”)

**Focus**: Questions can target the **current system** or the **desired new system**.

#### Checklist for Conducting an Interview

**Before**
- Establish objective
- Determine correct users and project team members
- Build list of questions
- Review related documents
- Set time and location

**During**
- Arrive on time
- Look for exception and error conditions
- Probe for details
- Take thorough notes
- Document open questions

**After**
- Review notes
- Transfer to models
- Identify areas for clarification
- Thank participants
- Follow up on open items

**Sample Interview Agenda** and **Open-Items List** are standard tools used to keep interviews organized.

### 2.2 Questionnaires

**Advantages**
- Suitable for collecting information from a **large number** of stakeholders (possibly in different locations)
- Often used to obtain **preliminary insight** before deeper research

**Disadvantages**
- Not suited to learn about **processes, workflows, or techniques**
- Open-ended questions may not be answered

**Best Use**: Early stage to gather broad information quickly.

### 2.3 Other Information-Gathering Techniques

- **Review Inputs, Outputs, and Procedures**
  - External sources: professional organizations, other companies, journals, magazines
  - Internal documents and procedures (serves two purposes)

- **Observe and Document Business Processes**
  - Gain first-hand experience via quick walk-through
  - Understand detailed work but **do not get locked** into the current process

- **Research Vendor Solutions**
  - See what others have done in similar situations
  - Less risky and often less costly

- **Collect User Comments and Suggestions**
  - Feedback on models and tests
  - Users know it when they see it

## 3. Models and Modelling

### Why Modelling is Important

After collecting information, analysts create models to define requirements. Key reasons:

- Learning from the modelling process
- Reducing complexity by abstraction
- Remembering all the details
- Communicating with other development team members
- Communicating with a variety of users and stakeholders
- Documenting what was done for future maintenance/enhancement

**A model** is a representation of some aspect of the system being built.

### Types of Models
- **Textual models** – written descriptions
- **Graphical models** – diagrams, schematics
- **Mathematical models** – formulas, statistics, algorithms

### Unified Modelling Language (UML)
- Standard graphical modelling language for information systems
- Provides consistent symbols and terminology

**Examples of common UML models**:
- Event list
- Use case diagram
- Use case description
- Location diagram
- Class diagram
- Sequence diagram
- Communication diagram
- State machine diagram

## 4. Documenting Workflows with Activity Diagrams

### Key Definitions

- **Workflow**: Sequence of processing steps that completely handles one business transaction or customer request.
- **Activity Diagram**: A UML diagram that describes:
  - User or system activities
  - The person/actor who performs each activity
  - The sequential (and parallel) flow of these activities

Activity diagrams are especially useful for showing a **graphical model of a workflow**.

### 4.1 Activity Diagram Symbols

| Symbol                        | Description                                      |
|-------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| Swimlane heading              | Represents an actor/agent (one per row)          |
| Starting activity (black dot) | Initial node (pseudo)                            |
| Activity                      | Rounded rectangle (main action)                  |
| Transition arrow              | Shows flow direction                             |
| Decision activity             | Diamond shape (yes/no or multiple paths)         |
| Synchronization bar (Split)   | Forks parallel paths                             |
| Synchronization bar (Join)    | Merges parallel paths back into one              |
| Ending activity (bullseye)    | Final node (pseudo)                              |

### 4.2 Examples of Activity Diagrams

1. **RMO Order Fulfillment** – Shows swimlanes for Order subsystem, Inventory subsystem, Warehouse, and Shipping company with decision points and synchronization.
2. **Activity Diagram with Concurrent Paths** – Demonstrates parallel activities across departments (Salesperson, Engineering, Purchasing, Production, Scheduling).

### 4.3 Steps to Develop an Activity Diagram

1. Identify the **agents** to create appropriate **swimlanes**.
2. Identify appropriate **ovals** (activities) in each swimlane.
3. Connect the ovals with **arrows**.
4. Use **decision symbols** and **synchronization bars** when needed:
   - Decision symbol → either/or situation (one path or the other, not both)
   - Synchronization bars → parallel paths (both paths are taken). Always include a beginning (split) and ending (join) bar.
