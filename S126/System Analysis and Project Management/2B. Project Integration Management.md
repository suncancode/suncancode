## 1. What is Project Integration Management?

Project Integration Management is the process of **coordinating all other knowlegde areas** throughout a project's entire life cycle. Think of the project manager as a conductor in an orchestra - each musician (scope, cost, risk, schedule) plays their own part, but the conductor ensures everyone plays together in harmony.

> Project Integration Management is **NOT** the same as software integration. It is a management concept, not a technical one.

Many new project managers struggle because they focus too heavily on technical details and lose sight of the **big picture**.

Each process has its own Inputs, Tools and Techniques, and Outputs (ITTOs) as defined in the PMBOK Guide.

## 2. Strategic Planning and Project Selection

Before a project begins, an organisation must ask: **Why are we doing this project?**

Strategic planning helps organisations determine long-term objectives by:
- Analysing internal strengths and weaknesses
- Studying external opportunities and threats
- Predicting future trends
- Projecting the need for new products and services

The most widely used tool is **SWOT Analysis**.

### SWOT Analysis
 
```
┌─────────────────────┬─────────────────────┐
│     STRENGTHS       │     WEAKNESSES      │
│  (Internal, +ve)    │   (Internal, -ve)   │
├─────────────────────┼─────────────────────┤
│   OPPORTUNITIES     │      THREATS        │
│  (External, +ve)    │   (External, -ve)   │
└─────────────────────┴─────────────────────┘
```
 
**Example — Film company SWOT:**
 
| Category | Items |
|----------|-------|
| Strengths | Industry contacts, sales skills, technical/filmmaking skills, strong portfolio |
| Weaknesses | No accounting experience, no marketing strategy, limited capital, no website |
| Opportunities | Potential large client project, growing film industry, two major conferences |
| Threats | Competition from established firms, high risk in film business |
 
**Projects identified from this SWOT:** hire an external accountant, build a company website, develop a marketing plan, submit a proposal for the client project, promote at conferences.
 
### IT Planning Pyramid

| Stage | Result |
|-------|--------|
| **IT Strategy Planning** | Align IT strategy to the organisation's mission and vision; identify key business areas |
| **Business Area Analysis** | Document business processes that could benefit from IT |
| **Project Planning** | Define potential projects, scope, benefits, and constraints |
| **Resource Allocation** | Select IT projects and assign resources |

## 3. Methos for Selecting Projects
 
Once potential projects are identified, they must be evaluated and prioritised. Five main methods are used.
 
### Method 1: Focusing on Broad Organisational Needs
 
Projects that address broad organisational needs are more likely to succeed. Three criteria must be satisfied:
 
| Criterion | Question |
|-----------|---------|
| **Need** | Do people in the organisation agree this project needs to be done? |
| **Funding** | Does the organisation have the capacity and desire to fund it adequately? |
| **Will** | Is there strong will to make the project succeed? |
 
### Method 2: Categorising IT Projects
 
Projects can be categorised by:
- **Impetus:** Is the project driven by a *Problem*, an *Opportunity*, or a *Directive*?
- **Time window:** How urgently is it needed?
- **Priority:** What is the overall relative importance?
 
### Method 3: Financial Analysis
 
#### a) Net Present Value (NPV)
The core idea: **$1 today is worth more than $1 in the future** because money can earn returns over time.
 
$$NPV = \sum_{t=0}^{n} \frac{A_t}{(1+r)^t}$$
 
Where:
- $A_t$ = cash flow in year $t$
- $r$ = discount rate
- $n$ = last year of the cash flow
 
**Rules:**
- NPV > 0 → project is financially viable; consider it
- When comparing projects → prefer the one with the **higher NPV**
- Note: two projects with equal total cash flows can have different NPVs due to the **time value of money**
 
**Example (10% discount rate):**
 
| | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Year 5 | Total |
|-|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|-------|
| Project 1 Cash Flow | -$5,000 | $1,000 | $2,000 | $3,000 | $4,000 | $5,000 |
| **NPV = $2,316** | | | | | | |
| Project 2 Cash Flow | -$1,000 | $0 | $2,000 | $2,000 | $2,000 | $5,000 |
| **NPV = $3,201** | | | | | | |
 
→ Project 2 has a higher NPV despite equal total cash flows, because its costs are lower in the early years.
 
#### b) Return on Investment (ROI)
 
$$ROI = \frac{\text{Total Discounted Benefits} - \text{Total Discounted Costs}}{\text{Discounted Costs}}$$
 
- The higher the ROI, the better
- Many organisations set a **minimum required rate of return** for acceptable projects
- **Internal Rate of Return (IRR)** = the discount rate at which NPV equals zero
 
#### c) Payback Analysis
 
The **payback period** is the time it takes for cumulative net benefits to equal total investment costs.
 
- Payback occurs when: cumulative discounted benefits = cumulative costs
- Shorter payback period = lower financial risk
- Many organisations set a **maximum acceptable payback period** as a selection criterion
 
### Method 4: Weighted Scoring Model
 
A systematic approach for evaluating projects against multiple criteria simultaneously.
 
**Steps:**
1. Identify relevant criteria for project selection
2. Assign a weight (%) to each criterion (weights must total 100%)
3. Score each project on each criterion (e.g., 0–100)
4. Multiply scores by weights and sum for a total weighted score
 
**Example criteria for IT projects:**
 
| Criteria | Weight | Project 1 | Project 2 | Project 3 | Project 4 |
|----------|--------|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Supports key business objectives | 25% | 90 | 90 | 50 | 20 |
| Has strong internal sponsor | 15% | 70 | 90 | 50 | 20 |
| Has strong customer support | 15% | 50 | 90 | 50 | 20 |
| Uses realistic technology | 10% | 25 | 90 | 50 | 70 |
| Implementable in one year or less | 5% | 20 | 20 | 50 | 90 |
| Provides positive NPV | 20% | 50 | 70 | 50 | 50 |
| Low risk (scope, time, cost) | 10% | 20 | 50 | 50 | 90 |
| **Total Weighted Score** | **100%** | **56** | **78.5** | **50** | **41.5** |
 
→ **Project 2** wins with the highest weighted score of 78.5.
 
### Method 5: Balanced Scorecard
 
Developed by Kaplan & Norton — a strategic planning and management system that helps organisations:
- Align business activities to overall strategy
- Improve internal and external communications
- Monitor organisational performance against strategic goals
 
More information: [https://www.balancedscorecard.org/](https://www.balancedscorecard.org/)

## 4. Developing a Project Charter

Once a project is selected, the first formal step is creating a **Project Charter**.

### What is a Project Charter?

> A document that **formally recognises the existence of a project** and provides direction on its objectives and management.

Key stakeholders must sign the charter to acknowledge agreement. It is the **key output of the Initiation phase**.

**Inputs for developing a project charter:**
- Business case
- Benefits management plan
- Agreements
- Enterprise environmental factors
- Organisational process assets

### Required Contents of a Project Charter
 
| Section | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Project title & date of authorisation | Formally identifies the project |
| Project Manager name & contact | Who is responsible |
| Summary schedule | Planned start and finish dates, key milestones |
| Budget summary | High-level cost estimate or reference to budget docs |
| Project objectives | Brief description and business justification |
| Success criteria | How success is defined and who approves |
| Management approach | Stakeholder needs, assumptions, constraints, references |
| Roles & Responsibilities matrix | Who does what |
| Sign-off section | Signatures of key stakeholders |
| Comments section | Stakeholder notes and concerns |
 
### Example: DNA-Sequencing Project Charter (Excerpt)
 
| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Project Title | Next-gen DNA-Sequencing Instrument Completion Project |
| Date of Authorisation | February 1 |
| Start / Finish Dates | February 1 / November 1 |
| Budget | $1.5 million (mostly internal labour; hardware outsourced) |
| Project Manager | Nick Carson |
| Sponsor | Ahmed Abrams (CEO) |
| Milestones | First software version by June 1; production version by November 1 |
| Success Criteria | Software meets all written specifications, thoroughly tested, completed on time. CEO signs off. |
| Approach | Hire technical replacement; develop WBS, scope statement and Gantt chart within one month; weekly progress meetings; thorough software testing |

## 5. Developing a Project Management Plan

The **Project Management Plan** is the document used to coordinate *all* planning documents and guide project execution and control. Where the charter answers "What and Why", the project mangement plan answers "How".

All subsidiary planns (scope, schedule, cost, risk, etc.) feed into this master document.

### Structure (based on IEEE Standard 1058)
 
| Section | Topics Covered |
|---------|---------------|
| **Overview** | Purpose, scope, objectives, assumptions, constraints, deliverables, schedule & budget summary |
| **Project Organisation** | External interfaces, internal structure, roles & responsibilities |
| **Managerial Process Plans** | Estimation, staffing, resource acquisition, work plan, schedule, control plan, risk management plan, closeout plan |
| **Technical Process Plans** | Process model, methods/tools/techniques, infrastructure plan, product acceptance plan |
| **Supporting Process Plans** | Configuration management, verification & validation, documentation, QA, problem resolution, subcontractor management, process improvement |
 
---

## 6. Directing and Managing Project Work

This phase involves **managing and performing** the work described in the project management plan.

Key points:
- The **majority of time and money** in a project is spent during execution
- The project manager's primary focus is **leading the team** and **managing stakeholder relationships**
- Planning and execution are **intertwined** - those who will do the work should help plan it
- All project personnel need both **planning and execution skills**

---

## 7. Monitoring and Controlling Project Work

Changes are inevitable - so a formal process for monitoring and control is essential.

Key concepts:
- **Monitoring** = collecting, measuring, and disseminating performance information
- **Baseline** = the original approved plan (scope, schedule, cost) used as a reference point for measuring deviations
- The project management plan provides the baseline for identifying and controlling changes
- **Integrated Change Controll** ensures all changes are reviewed, approved, and documented before being implemented

---

## 8. Closing Projects or Phases

Closing finalises all activities and transfers the completed (or cancelled) work to the appropriate parties.

**Main inputs:** project charter, project management plan, project documents, accepted deliverables, business documents, agreements, procurement documentation, organisational process assets.
 
**Main tools:** expert judgment, data analysis, meetings.
 
**Key outputs:** final product/service/result transition, final report, lessons learned register, organisational process assets updates.

## 9. Case Study — Nick Carson
 
### Background
Nick Carson became PM of a critical DNA-sequencing project at a Silicon Valley biotech company. The project had already failed under three previous PMs. The CEO demanded: first version in **4 months**, production version in **9 months**.
 
### What Happened
Nick had strong technical skills and identified critical flaws in the instrument. He and his team delivered the product on time. However, **top management was still unhappy**.
 
### Why?
Nick focused entirely on technical problem-solving (software integrator/troubleshooter) and **neglected his responsibilities as project manager**:
 
| PM Responsibility | Nick's Failure |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Planning | Never produced accurate schedules or detailed project plans |
| Communicating | Did not keep management informed of project status |
| Reporting | Did not provide performance information or progress reports |
| Stakeholder Management | Ignored management's need for visibility and control |
 
### Lesson Learned
 
> **Delivering the product is not enough.** A project manager must manage the *entire* project — planning, communication, documentation, and stakeholder management — not just solve technical problems.
 
Nick confused the role of **technical lead** with the role of **project manager**. These are fundamentally different responsibilities.
