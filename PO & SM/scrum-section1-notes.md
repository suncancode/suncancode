## Lesson 5: Agile History & Explanation

### Why Was Agile Created?

Before Agile, software projects used the **Waterfall** methodology:

```
Requirements → Design → Development → Testing → Deployment
```

**Problems with Waterfall:**
- Projects took 1–2 years before delivering anything
- Changing requirements mid-project caused massive rework
- Bugs were discovered very late, making fixes extremely costly

### The Agile Manifesto (2001)

In 2001, **17 software experts** met in Snowbird, Utah, USA and wrote the **Agile Manifesto** — establishing 4 core values:

| We value... | Over... |
|---|---|
| **Individuals and interactions** | Processes and tools |
| **Working software** | Comprehensive documentation |
| **Customer collaboration** | Contract negotiation |
| **Responding to change** | Following a plan |

> 💡 **Agile is NOT a framework** — it is a **mindset**. Scrum is a framework that implements Agile thinking.

---

### The 12 Principles of Agile

The Agile Manifesto also includes **12 supporting principles**:

1. **Customer satisfaction** — Our highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.
2. **Welcome change** — Welcome changing requirements, even late in development. Agile processes harness change for the customer's competitive advantage.
3. **Deliver frequently** — Deliver working software frequently, from a couple of weeks to a couple of months, with a preference for the shorter timescale.
4. **Collaborate daily** — Business people and developers must work together daily throughout the project.
5. **Motivated individuals** — Build projects around motivated individuals. Give them the environment and support they need, and trust them to get the job done.
6. **Face-to-face conversation** — The most efficient and effective method of conveying information to and within a development team is face-to-face conversation.
7. **Working software is the measure** — Working software is the primary measure of progress.
8. **Sustainable pace** — Agile processes promote sustainable development. The sponsors, developers, and users should be able to maintain a constant pace indefinitely.
9. **Technical excellence** — Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility.
10. **Simplicity** — The art of maximizing the amount of work not done is essential.
11. **Self-organizing teams** — The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organizing teams.
12. **Reflect and adjust** — At regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behavior accordingly.

## Lesson 6: Scrum History

- **1986** — The term "Scrum" first appeared in a paper by **Takeuchi & Nonaka**, inspired by the **Rugby scrum** formation
- **1995** — **Jeff Sutherland & Ken Schwaber** formalized the Scrum framework
- **2010** — The first official **Scrum Guide** was published (updated periodically; latest version: **2020**)

## Lesson 7: Scrum in Under 5 Minutes

### What is Scrum?

Scrum is a framework that helps teams **deliver product value in short cycles** called **Sprints**.

### Scrum Flow:

```
📋 Product Backlog
        ↓
🗓️  Sprint Planning
        ↓
⚡  Sprint (1–4 weeks)
    ├── Daily Scrum (15 min/day)
    └── Sprint Backlog
        ↓
✅  Sprint Review
        ↓
🔄  Sprint Retrospective
        ↓
🚀  Product Increment (shippable product)
```

### 3 Roles in Scrum:

| Role | Main Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Product Owner** | Manages Product Backlog, prioritizes features |
| **Scrum Master** | Supports the team, removes impediments |
| **Developers** | Builds the product increment |

## Lesson 10: The Scrum Definition

> *"Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps people, teams, and organizations generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems."*
> — **Scrum Guide 2020**

### 3 Pillars of Scrum (Empiricism):

| Pillar | Description |
|---|---|
| 🔍 **Transparency** | Everyone has a clear view of the work and progress |
| 🔎 **Inspection** | Regularly inspect artifacts and progress toward goals |
| 🔧 **Adaptation** | Adjust processes when deviations are detected |

## Lesson 11: The Scrum Values

Scrum is built on **5 core values**:

| Value | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Commitment** | The team commits to achieving Sprint goals |
| **Focus** | Everyone focuses on the work of the Sprint |
| **Openness** | Be transparent about progress and obstacles |
| **Respect** | Team members respect each other's skills and effort |
| **Courage** | Have courage to do the right thing and speak the truth |

## Lessons 12–15: Product Owner in Scrum

### Lesson 12 — Workflow

- Collaborate with stakeholders to gather requirements
- Write **User Stories** and add them to the **Product Backlog**
- **Prioritize** backlog items based on business value
- Ensure the team always works on the highest-value items first

### Lesson 13 — WIP (Work In Progress)

- The PO limits the number of items being worked on simultaneously
- Keeping WIP low prevents the team from being overloaded
- Fewer items in progress = higher quality output

### Lesson 14 — Value

- The PO constantly asks: *"What value does this feature deliver to the user?"*
- Features are prioritized by **highest ROI (Return on Investment)**
- Value can be: revenue, user satisfaction, risk reduction, or compliance

### Lesson 15 — Trade-offs

- The PO must make **trade-off decisions**: Scope vs Time vs Quality
- The classic constraint triangle: you can't have everything
- The PO's job is to choose what matters most at any given moment
